== What you built

* You *analyzed* it with the Migration Toolkit for Applications (MTA) 8 and got a real number: *24 story points*, *6 mandatory* blockers, plus a technology inventory.
* You *triaged* the report like a consultant — grouping seven findings into ~three pieces of work.
* You *fixed* the hardcoded-datasource issue with *Developer Lightspeed for MTA* proposing the diff (`System.getenv("CLAIMS_DB_URL")`) — which *you reviewed* — or by hand where the entitlement is absent.
* You *re-analyzed* and watched the effort *drop*, then *deployed* the modernized service, breaking and fixing a health probe on the way.

The through-line: *modernization is a measured loop* — analyze, fix, re-analyze — not a leap of faith.

== Map it to your org

Discuss with the person next to you:

. *Take one app you own.* What are its "baked-in" assumptions — a hardcoded host, a filesystem path, a required app server, a specific JDK? Which would MTA mark *mandatory*, and which would you *retain* or *retire* instead of fix?
. *Who reviews the AI's diffs?* If your team adopts Developer Lightspeed (or any AI refactoring), what is the *review gate* — who reads the proposal, and how do you *prove* the fix (a test? a re-analysis?) before it merges?
. *What's your funnel?* If you had 50 apps to modernize, how would you *rank* them — and what would make an app a "start here" (low effort, high value) versus a "not this year" (high effort, low value)?

== When NOT to use this

Honesty keeps this credible — modernization is not always the move:

* *Don't modernize what you should retire.* If an app is being replaced or turned off within the year, spend zero effort on it. *Retire* and *retain* are legitimate Rs.
* *Don't rewrite when you can replatform.* The big-bang refactor is where modernization projects die. If externalizing config and containerizing (replatform) gets you 80% of the value, *stop there* — a full refactor to a new runtime is a separate, later decision.
* *Don't trust an AI diff you didn't read.* Developer Lightspeed accelerates the typing, not the accountability. For a security-sensitive change, a subtle logic change, or anything touching money, the review bar is *higher*, not waived. If you can't review it, don't accept it.
* *Don't treat the effort score as a deadline.* Story points are a *relative* estimate for *ranking and burn-down*, not a promise of hours. Use them to compare apps and track progress, not to commit a date to a VP.
* *MTA analyzes, it doesn't run your tests.* A clean report means the *migration rules* pass, not that the app *works*. Your test suite is still the source of truth for behavior.

== The bigger journey — the strangler pattern

You modernized *one* service. The portfolio play is the *funnel* — assess, analyze, fix the high-value/low-effort candidates, retain/retire the rest — run continuously, not as a one-time project.

For a large monolith, the incremental path is the *strangler pattern*: stand the modern service up *beside* the legacy one and move traffic to it slice by slice until the old app is "strangled" — off, with no big-bang cutover. That coexistence is a *connectivity* problem: routing a fraction of live traffic to a new service, often across clusters or sites, is exactly what *Service Interconnect* provides — covered in Resilience, Multi-Cluster & DR (a separate module). MTA tells you *what* to build next; the strangler pattern and L7 connectivity let you ship it *without a flag day*.
