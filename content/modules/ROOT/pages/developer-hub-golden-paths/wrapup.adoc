== What you built

You stepped into a new Parasol engineer's first hour and used the platform team's *developer portal* to go from nothing to a running, standards-compliant service.
You toured the *software catalog* — the org's live map — and read what it knows about `parasol-claims`: its owner (`parasol`), its System (`parasol-insurance`), and the API it provides, all sourced from a file that lives with the code.
Then you ran a *golden-path template*: a short form scaffolded a complete Quarkus (Java 21) service into your *own* Git organization, published the repository, and registered it in the catalog — where it immediately appeared as part of the `parasol-insurance` System.
You inspected the paved road (probes, metrics, tracing, a trusted UBI9 image, a build recipe, a workspace — none hand-written), then built its image in-cluster and ran it in `{user}-dev`, watching `/api/info`, `/q/health/ready`, and `/q/metrics` all answer.

The idea under all of it: *a portal turns tribal knowledge into a self-service road.*
The catalog answers "what do we run and who owns it" from files next to the code, and the golden path lets a new engineer start something new — correctly, with every standard from the rest of Block B already applied — without asking anyone.

== Map to your org

. *How does a new service get created in your organization today — and who has to be in the room?*
You created one from a form in minutes.
Think about your last new service: was it scaffolded from a template the platform team owns, or copied from an existing repo (including whatever was wrong with it) and then fixed up in review?
How many people, tickets, and days sat between "we need a service" and "it is running with probes, a trusted image, and an owner recorded"?
. *If someone asks "who owns this and what depends on it," where do they look?*
You answered ownership, System, and API from the catalog in seconds.
In your org, is that a query against a live map generated from code — or an archaeology project across a wiki, a spreadsheet, and Slack, current as of nobody-knows-when?
What breaks during an incident when the map is stale?
. *Which of your standards are enforced by construction, and which are enforced by audit?*
The golden path made new services start compliant.
Pick a standard you care about — a required label, a base image, a health probe.
Is it something new services *have* because the template gave it to them, or something you *find missing later* and file a ticket to add?

== When *not* to reach for this

A developer portal is powerful, and like every capability in this workshop it earns its place only sometimes.
Be honest about the edges:

* *Don't stand up a portal you will not feed.*
A catalog is only as useful as it is current, and it stays current only if entities are described in code and owned.
A portal that nobody maintains rots into the stale wiki it was meant to replace — now with more infrastructure to run.
If you have three services and one team, a portal is overhead; reach for it when *cognitive load and coordination cost* are the real problem, not before.
* *Don't let golden paths sprawl.*
One paved road is leverage; twenty subtly different templates are the copy-and-drift problem you were escaping, moved up a level.
Keep the set of templates small and opinionated, and retire the ones nobody uses.
* *Don't turn the paved road into a cage.*
If the template is the *only* allowed way and it cannot accommodate a legitimate need, teams route around it — shadow repos, hand-rolled services, the portal ignored.
A golden path works because the standard thing is the *easy* thing, not the *only* thing; leave room to drive off the road with a reason.
* *Don't platform-engineer every workflow.*
Not everything deserves a template.
A one-off, a spike, an experiment, or a genuinely bespoke system is clearer created by hand than forced through a golden path that hides it inside a form.
Templatize the *common* path — the tenth service that looks like the last nine — and leave the exceptions alone.
* *The portal is production software.*
Red Hat Developer Hub is a real application you deploy, upgrade, secure, and back up.
That is a fair cost when it removes cognitive load for a whole engineering org; it is a poor trade when it exists to serve a handful of services that were not confused to begin with.

== Go deeper

* *Ways to Build & Deliver Apps*, *Pipelines*, *Trusted Software Supply Chain*, and *GitOps* — the modules whose decisions a golden path *encodes*: the golden path is only as good as the base image, pipeline, and GitOps wiring the platform team standardized in those modules (separate modules).
* *Dev Spaces & the Inner Loop* — the scaffolded service ships a `devfile.yaml`; "Open in Dev Spaces" lands you in a ready Java 21 workspace to make your first change (a separate module).
* *Authoring your own golden path* — a Software Template is a form (`spec.parameters`) plus steps (`spec.steps`: `fetch:template`, `publish:gitea`, `catalog:register`), with the skeleton it stamps out living beside it in Git.
Improving the skeleton once improves every future service — the platform team's highest-leverage edit.
* *Developer Lightspeed for Red Hat Developer Hub* — an AI assistant that can answer questions about your services and the platform from inside the portal, an *Advanced Developer Suite* capability a platform team can enable on top of the portal (distinct from *OpenShift Lightspeed*, the console assistant).
* Red Hat documentation: the *Red Hat Developer Hub* guides for version {rhdh_version} — the software catalog, authentication, configuring dynamic plugins, and authoring Software Templates.
