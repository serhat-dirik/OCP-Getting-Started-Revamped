[.lead]
Parasol just failed a supplier security audit: nobody could prove which build produced the claims image running in production, or what was inside it.

The pipeline from the previous work builds and deploys `parasol-claims` reliably — but "it builds" is not the same as "it is trustworthy."
The auditor asked three questions Parasol could not answer: _what dependencies are in this image, who built it, and can you prove the running image is the one your pipeline produced?_
In this module you add the three controls that answer those questions — a *vulnerability scan gate*, *image signing with provenance*, and a *software bill of materials* — and you make the cluster *refuse to run* anything that is not signed.

[NOTE]
.What "[ADS]" means, and what is actually included
====
This module uses *Red Hat Advanced Cluster Security for Kubernetes* (RHACS) and *Red Hat Trusted Artifact Signer* (RHTAS) — part of the *Red Hat Advanced Developer Suite* (RHADS), a separate subscription from a base OpenShift subscription.
RHACS is *also* included with OpenShift Platform Plus.
Two of the controls you use, though — *Tekton Chains* image signing and the native *`ImagePolicy`* admission gate — ship with OpenShift itself, so part of the trust story is included the moment you have Pipelines.
The lab flags which control is which as you go.
====

== What you'll learn

* The *supply-chain threat model* in plain words — "you ship your dependencies" — and what the *SLSA* levels actually ask of you.

== You ship your dependencies

The single idea under this whole module: *the code your team wrote is a small fraction of what you deploy.*
The `parasol-claims` image is mostly other people's code — the Quarkus runtime, its transitive libraries, the base operating-system layers — pulled in at build time.
That is normal and good; it is also the attack surface.
When a critical flaw is found in a widely used library (Log4Shell in `log4j-core`, in December 2021, is the canonical example), the question is not "did we write the bug" — it is *"is it in anything we ship, and can we find out in minutes?"*

A trusted software supply chain answers three questions about every artifact you build:

Provenance:: _Where did this come from?_ Who built it, from which source, with which builder — and can you prove the running image is that exact build, not a substitute.
Integrity:: _Has it been tampered with?_ A cryptographic *signature* that the image you run is byte-for-byte the image your pipeline produced.
Contents:: _What is inside?_ A *software bill of materials* (SBOM) — the itemized list of every component and version — so that when the next Log4Shell lands, "are we affected?" is a query, not an archaeology project.

[TIP]
.SLSA, in one paragraph
====
*SLSA* (Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts, pronounced "salsa") is an industry framework that grades _how trustworthy your build process is_ — not your code, your *process*.
The levels climb roughly like this: *build on a hosted system* (not a laptop), *produce signed provenance* (the build system attests what it did), and *make that provenance tamper-resistant and complete*.
You do not need to memorize the level numbers.
What matters is the shape: a pipeline that builds on the cluster, signs what it produces, and attaches machine-readable provenance is *already* climbing SLSA — and that is exactly what Tekton Chains gives you in this module for free.
====

== The signature / attestation / SBOM triangle

Three artifacts travel with a trustworthy image, and they answer three *different* questions.
People conflate them constantly, so pin the distinction now:

[cols="1,3,2",options="header"]
|===
| Artifact | Answers | Produced by (in this lab)

| *Signature* | "This image is intact and came from us." A cryptographic signature over the image digest. | Tekton Chains (cosign key)
| *Attestation (provenance)* | "Here is _how_ this image was built" — the builder, the source, the steps, the timestamps. A signed *SLSA provenance* statement. | Tekton Chains (in-toto / SLSA)
| *SBOM* | "Here is _what is inside_" — every component and version. | The build (CycloneDX)
|===

A signature with no SBOM tells you the image is authentic but not what is in it.
An SBOM with no signature tells you what _a_ build contained but not that _this_ image is that build.
You need all three, and they reinforce each other: the attestation is _signed_, the SBOM can be _attested_, and the signature is what an admission gate _checks_.

== Policy as a gate, and policy as an admission control

There are two places to stop a bad image, and they are not interchangeable — a mature supply chain uses *both*.

Policy-as-gate (build time)::
A *scan gate* runs inside the pipeline: build the image, scan it, and *fail the build* if it violates policy.
This is where you catch a vulnerable dependency _before_ the image is ever published.
In the lab, RHACS runs `roxctl image check` as a pipeline task; a critical CVE returns a non-zero exit code, the task fails, and the pipeline stops.
The gate's value is *shift-left*: the developer who introduced the flaw finds out in their own pipeline run, not in production.

Policy-as-admission (run time)::
A gate only helps if _every_ image goes through the pipeline.
*Admission* closes that gap by enforcing a rule at the cluster: OpenShift's native `ImagePolicy` tells the node to *verify a signature before it will pull an image* into a namespace.
An unsigned image — one that never went through your signing pipeline — is *refused at pull time*.
The gate is a policy you _run_; admission is a policy the platform _enforces_ whether or not anyone remembered to run the gate.

[NOTE]
.Why the scan gate keys on a specific policy — determinism
====
A default RHACS build policy like "Fixable Severity at least Important" fires on *any* fixable CVE in the image — including base-operating-system CVEs that appear and disappear as the vulnerability feed updates.
That is correct for a real security program, but it makes a _teaching_ gate non-deterministic: the same image can pass on Monday and fail on Friday.
So the lab's gate is scoped to a *single policy category* ("Supply Chain Security") containing one deterministic, enforced policy — "Block Log4Shell (CVE-2021-44228) at build."
The gate then fires on exactly one thing: the seeded `log4j-core` dependency.
Remove that dependency and the gate goes green — reliably, every time.
====

== Red Hat trusted content and the base image

The base image is the largest single source of what you ship, and it is the thread from the earlier build module.
`parasol-claims` builds `FROM` a Red Hat *Universal Base Image* (UBI) OpenJDK layer.
Trusted content matters here for two reasons.
First, *provenance*: UBI images are built and signed by Red Hat, so the bottom of your image has a supply chain you did not have to assemble.
Second, *currency*: a UBI tag is patched over time, so an _older_ tag carries known base-OS CVEs that a _newer_ tag has fixed.
The lab's seeded image pins an older UBI tag on purpose — bumping it is *complementary hardening* (fewer base-OS CVEs), and the lab treats it as exactly that: a good habit, but *not* the thing that turns the scan gate green.
The gate is about the vulnerable dependency you _added_; the base image is about keeping the foundation current.

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Are we affected by <the CVE that just broke the internet>?" becomes a query.*
Before SBOMs, that question triggers a fire drill: every team greps their build files by hand.
After, you search your stored bills of materials for the component and get a list of affected images in minutes.
The SBOM is boring infrastructure until the day it saves you a weekend.
* *The pipeline is where security shifts left.*
A scan gate in the pipeline means the person who added the vulnerable dependency is the person who sees it fail — in their own run, with the CVE named.
That is far cheaper than a security team finding it in production and filing a ticket back to a developer who has moved on to something else.
* *Signing is only half the story; enforcement is the other half.*
Plenty of organizations sign their images and then never check the signature at run time.
Admission control (`ImagePolicy` here, or RHACS's cluster-wide admission) is what turns "we sign our images" into "the cluster will not run an unsigned one" — the difference between a policy on a wiki and a policy the platform enforces.
* *Provenance is what an auditor actually asks for.*
"Show me that the image in production came from this commit through this pipeline" is a real audit question.
A signed SLSA attestation is a machine-checkable answer — and because Tekton Chains produces it automatically, you get the answer without anyone writing it up.

.Where this sits relative to the pipeline and GitOps modules
====
The previous module gave you a pipeline that produces an image and *data* (results).
This module adds the *trust* controls to that pipeline — a scan that can fail the build, a signature and provenance on every image, and an SBOM.
The GitOps modules that follow take the *trustworthy, signed* image this pipeline produces and reconcile it into each environment — so admission control (only-signed-runs) and GitOps promotion are the two halves of "the right image, and only the right image, reaches production" (both are separate modules).
