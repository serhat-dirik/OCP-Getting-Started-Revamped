The one idea under all of it: *admission control is what makes a batch tier fair at scale*, and an AI inference job is admitted by exactly the same machinery as a statement run — which is why the whole industry is standardizing GPU scheduling on it.

== Choosing the right tool: Job vs Tekton vs Serverless

Batch is one of three ways to run work that isn't a long-lived server, and picking the wrong one is a common, expensive mistake.
Use this guide:

[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Tool | Use it when | Not when

| *Job / CronJob* (this module)
| A *bounded* body of work runs to completion and stops — process a dataset, generate statements, score a batch, run on a nightly schedule. One step (or a simple parallel fan-out), no inter-step artifacts.
| The work is a multi-step *pipeline* with dependencies and hand-offs (use Tekton), or it reacts to individual events and should scale to zero between them (use Serverless).

| *Tekton / OpenShift Pipelines*
| Work is a *sequence of steps* with ordering, shared *workspaces*, passed *results*, and gates — build → test → scan → deploy. You want per-step logs, retries, and a visual graph.
| It is a single bounded computation with no steps — that is a Job, and wrapping it in a Pipeline is overhead.

| *Serverless / Knative*
| Work is *event-* or *request-driven* and bursty, and should *scale to zero* when idle — an HTTP endpoint, a per-message handler, a webhook.
| The work is a bounded batch that runs once and stops — Knative keeps *reconciling to a desired state*; a Job is the right "run once, then done" shape.
|===

The short version: *Jobs run to completion; Pipelines orchestrate steps; Serverless reacts to events.*
Batch inference is a Job (it runs over a dataset and stops); a model *serving* endpoint is Serverless (it answers requests and scales to zero); a model *build-and-deploy* flow is a Pipeline.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams run batch today:

. *Who owns the batch quota?*
You watched a ClusterQueue decide which jobs ran and which waited — that queue is a *policy*, and policies have owners.
In your org, who decides how much of the cluster the batch tier may use, and how competing teams share it?
If the answer is "whoever submits the biggest job wins," you have found a platform-team backlog item with a real dollar value.
. *Which of your CronJobs can you name the owner of?*
Estates accumulate CronJobs the way garages accumulate boxes — dozens of them, half overlapping, some that should have become event-driven the day a queue appeared.
Pick one scheduled job you run in production: can you name its owner, its `concurrencyPolicy`, and what happens when a run overruns the next tick?
If not, that is a 2&nbsp;a.m. page waiting to happen.
. *Where will your first GPU-contention fight happen?*
The first time two teams want the same accelerators at the same time, someone invents an admission policy — usually a spreadsheet and an argument.
You just saw that policy as code.
For your AI roadmap, who arbitrates GPU access, and would you rather design that queue now or during the crunch?

== When *not* to reach for this

Batch and admission control are powerful, and they are not always the right call.
Be honest about the edges:

* *Don't put Kueue in front of a three-job team.*
Admission control earns its keep when *many* teams contend for *scarce* capacity.
If one team runs a handful of jobs on a cluster with headroom, a namespace `ResourceQuota` is enough — a ClusterQueue, priorities, and preemption are governance you will not use, and every attendee here got their own queue precisely because sharing one is what creates contention.
Add Kueue when contention is real, not before.
* *Don't cron what should be event-driven.*
A CronJob that polls every minute for "did a file arrive" is a queue-shaped problem wearing a schedule.
If the trigger is an *event* — a message, an upload, a webhook — an event-driven handler (Serverless, or a queue consumer) reacts instantly and costs nothing while idle, instead of waking every minute to find nothing.
Reach for a schedule only when the trigger genuinely *is* the clock.
* *Don't run a Job where a Deployment belongs (or vice-versa).*
A Job that never ends (an infinite `sleep`, a server loop) is a Deployment in disguise — you lose health/rollout semantics and confuse the scheduler.
A Deployment doing a one-shot migration on every start is a Job in disguise — it re-runs on every restart.
Match the object to the lifecycle: *runs forever* → Deployment; *runs once, then done* → Job.

* *OpenShift Pipelines (Tekton)* — the multi-step counterpart to Jobs, for build/test/deploy workflows with dependencies and artifacts (a separate module in this workshop).
* *OpenShift Serverless (Knative)* — the event-driven, scale-to-zero counterpart, for request- and event-shaped work (a separate module).
