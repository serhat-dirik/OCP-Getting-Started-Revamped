The one idea under all of it: the image carries the *behavior*, and the environment supplies the *settings*.
Get that split right and "promote to production" becomes applying config to bytes you already tested — not a rebuild, and not a surprise.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams work today:

. *Where does your configuration actually live?*
List one real service and count the places its configuration hides: baked into the image, in environment variables, in a config file on a server, in someone's head.
How many of those would survive the person who set them up leaving?
What would it take to make the environment's config a reviewed change in Git instead?
. *Who can read your production database password right now?*
A Kubernetes Secret separates "can deploy" from "can read credentials" — but only if you use that separation.
For a real system of yours, ask: can everyone who can deploy also read the production secrets?
Should they?
Where is the *source of truth* for that credential, and who rotates it?
. *How different are your environments, really?*
You proved dev, stage, and prod can run the identical image and differ only by declared config.
For your own pipeline: how much of the difference between your staging and production is *configuration you could diff* — and how much is drift nobody can fully account for?
Every bit you move into the first column is a class of "worked in staging" bug you retire.

== When *not* to do it this way

Externalized configuration is a strong default, but every tool here can be over-applied.
Be honest about the edges:

* *Env-var sprawl is a real smell.*
Environment variables are perfect for a handful of values.
When a service accumulates *dozens* of them, they become as hard to manage as the hardcoding you replaced — no structure, no types, no grouping.
That is the signal to move to a *mounted config file* (a whole properties or YAML document) or, at real scale, a dedicated configuration service.
Do not let "externalize everything into env vars" become its own mess.
* *A Secret is not a vault.*
Base64 is encoding, not encryption, and a Kubernetes Secret sits in the cluster datastore readable by anyone with the right RBAC.
For low-stakes workshop credentials that is fine; for regulated data, PCI, or anything that would make the news, a Secret alone is *not* sufficient.
That is where the External Secrets Operator and a real manager (Vault, a cloud secrets service) earn their place — do not mistake a Secret for security it does not provide.
* *Three overlays for a two-setting app is over-engineering.*
The base-plus-overlays pattern shines when environments genuinely diverge and change often.
A small internal tool with one environment does not need a Kustomize base, three overlays, and a promotion flow — that machinery is cost you pay for flexibility you are not using.
Reach for it when the number of environments or the rate of config change justifies it, not by default.
* *Probes can lie if you point them at the wrong thing.*
A readiness probe that checks something trivial (the process is up) tells the platform the pod is ready when it is not; a liveness probe that checks a *dependency* (the database) can restart a healthy pod during a blip and turn a small outage into a restart storm.
Probe *your own* readiness — can I serve? — not your dependencies' health.
