== What you built

You took Parasol's claims platform from "GitOps-managed" to "GitOps at scale, delivered safely."
You replaced the hand-made per-environment Applications with *one ApplicationSet* — a list generator that stamped out `dev`, `stage`, and `prod` from a single template, adopting the two that existed and standing up production.
You watched production converge in the right order through *sync waves and a migration hook* — database, then migration, then app.
You shipped a new version to production as a *canary* that earned its traffic in slices (25% → pause → 50% → analysis → 100%), gated by an *automated analysis* check.
And you shipped a bad release on purpose and watched the canary *abort and roll back on its own* at the failed analysis step — production answering `200` the entire time.

The idea under all of it: *at scale, the fleet is generated, and delivery is measured.*
One object describes many Applications so standards apply by construction; and production takes a new version in slices an analysis gate can reject, so a bad release rolls back on its own before it reaches anyone.

== Map to your org

. *How many hand-made deployment definitions are drifting in your org right now?*
You generated three Applications from one template.
Pick a service that runs in several environments (or several clusters): is each environment a hand-maintained copy that has quietly diverged, or is it generated from one source the platform team owns?
What would it take to make "onboard a new service or environment" mean "one line of generator input," not "copy and edit N files"?
. *When your last bad release reached production, how did you find out — and how long until it was reverted?*
You watched a canary catch a bad build on evidence and roll back automatically.
Think of a real incident that started with a deploy that "looked fine": would a canary with an analysis gate have caught it before full rollout, and how much of the mean-time-to-recovery was a human noticing and deciding?
. *What is your "run the migration in the right place" rule, and where does it live?*
You encoded database → migration → app as sync waves and a hook, in the same repo as the app.
In your org, is that ordering a runbook step a human can skip, or is it structural — and what breaks when someone forgets it?

== When *not* to reach for this

Progressive delivery and ApplicationSets are powerful, which means being honest about their edges:

* *Don't put a canary on a change a canary cannot judge.*
A canary is only as good as its analysis.
A schema change, a data migration, a change whose failure mode is *slow* or *downstream* (a subtle data corruption, a cache that fills over hours) will pass a short readiness-based analysis and still be wrong.
Canary the changes whose health you can *measure in the bake window*; gate the rest on tests, review, and manual promotion.
* *Don't reach for pod-ratio canary when you need exact or rule-based traffic.*
This module split traffic by *pod ratio* — a quarter of pods, so roughly a quarter of requests.
If you need *exactly* 5% of requests, header- or user-based routing, or mirrored/shadow traffic, that needs a *traffic router* (an OpenShift Route plugin, or a service mesh — separate modules), not a Rollout alone.
* *Don't ApplicationSet-generate what genuinely wants to be hand-held.*
An ApplicationSet is for a *set of like things*.
A one-of-a-kind Application with bespoke config, a sensitive singleton, or something mid-migration that you want to change and inspect by hand is clearer as its own Application — generating it buys nothing and hides it inside a template.
* *Don't let auto-promotion outrun your confidence.*
Automated canary promotion is a feature in a mature pipeline with trustworthy signals and a *tested* rollback — and a foot-gun before you have those.
Start with a *manual* promotion gate (or blue-green with a human cutover), earn trust in the analysis, and automate promotion only once the gate has proven it catches real regressions.
* *Blue-green costs double capacity for the cutover window.*
Two full copies of production is real quota.
When the environment is large or expensive, a canary (a small extra slice) is kinder to your budget than blue-green (a whole second copy) — pick the strategy your capacity can actually afford.

== Go deeper

* *Networking for Dev & DevOps* and *Service Mesh Zero-to-Hero* — for *request-level* traffic shifting (exact percentages, header routing, mirroring) the pod-ratio canary here cannot do, via an OpenShift Route traffic-router plugin or Istio routing (separate modules).
* *Serverless Zero-to-Hero* — a *third* way to shift traffic: Knative revisions with tag-based routing, for workloads that scale to zero (a separate module).
* *Business Continuity & Multi-Cluster* — the *cluster* generator turns the ApplicationSet shape you learned here into "this app on every cluster," the foundation of multi-cluster GitOps with Advanced Cluster Management (a separate module).
* *GitOps Fundamentals* — the reconcile loop, Applications, drift and self-heal that this module builds on (a separate module).
* Red Hat documentation: the *OpenShift GitOps* guide for OpenShift {ocp_version} (GitOps {gitops_version} / Argo CD {argocd_version}) — ApplicationSets and generators, sync waves and resource hooks, and *Argo Rollouts* (canary, blue-green, AnalysisTemplates).

.Clean up
====
Leave everything running if you are continuing — nothing depends on tearing it down.
When you want a clean entry state, run `ws prep {module-id} --yes`: it purges `{user}-dev`, `{user}-stage`, `{user}-prod`, restores your `claims-config` fork, and *removes the ApplicationSet before the Applications it generated* — so nothing gets re-created behind the reset (dev/stage return to plain Applications, prod goes empty).
To remove just the ApplicationSet yourself, `~/argocd appset delete claims-{user}` deletes it and prunes its three Applications in one step.
