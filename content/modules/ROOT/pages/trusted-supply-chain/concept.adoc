[.lead]
Parasol just failed a supplier security audit: nobody could prove which build produced the claims image running in production, or what was inside it.

The pipeline from the previous work builds and deploys `parasol-claims` reliably — but "it builds" is not the same as "it is trustworthy."
The auditor asked three questions Parasol could not answer: _what dependencies are in this image, who built it, and can you prove the running image is the one your pipeline produced?_
In this module you add the controls that answer those questions — a *software bill of materials* (what is inside), *image signing with SLSA provenance* (who built it, and proof the running image is intact), and *signature admission* — so the cluster will *refuse to run* anything that is not signed.

[NOTE]
.What "[ADS]" means, and what is actually included
====
This module uses *Red Hat Advanced Cluster Security for Kubernetes* (RHACS) and *Red Hat Trusted Artifact Signer* (RHTAS) — part of the *Red Hat Advanced Developer Suite* (RHADS), a separate subscription from a base OpenShift subscription.
RHACS is *also* included with OpenShift Platform Plus.
Two of the controls you use, though — *Tekton Chains* image signing and the native *`ImagePolicy`* admission gate — ship with OpenShift itself, so part of the trust story is included the moment you have Pipelines.
The lab flags which control is which as you go.
====

[NOTE]
.Two halves of DevSecOps — scanning and trust
====
*Scanning* your code and images for known flaws — SAST (static analysis), SCA (dependency CVEs), DAST (the running app), configuration, and image risk — is its own module, *Application Security Testing* (a separate module).
*This* module is the other half: proving *provenance* and *signing and verifying* what you ship, so only *trusted* artifacts run.
Put plainly: that module asks _"is it free of known flaws?"_; this one asks _"is it provably ours?"_ — and a mature pipeline does *both*.
The pipeline here includes a scan step only as *context* — the place trust controls attach — so the scan _technique_ stays the other module's subject.
====

== What you'll learn

* The *supply-chain threat model* in plain words — "you ship your dependencies" — and what the *SLSA* (Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts) levels actually ask of your build _process_.
* The *signature / attestation / SBOM* (software bill of materials) *triangle*: three different facts about an image, and why you need all three.
* How *Tekton Chains* signs every pipeline-built image and emits *SLSA provenance* — with no per-build wiring — and how you verify it with `cosign`.
* The difference between a *build-time gate* (fail the build) and *run-time admission* (refuse to run) — and how a native OpenShift `ImagePolicy` enforces "only signed images run," so a signature is *checked*, not merely produced.
* Where trust ends and *scanning* begins: catching flaws with SAST, SCA, DAST, and image risk is a separate module (*Application Security Testing*); here you prove *provenance* and *sign and verify* what you ship.

== You ship your dependencies

The single idea under this whole module: *the code your team wrote is a small fraction of what you deploy.*
The `parasol-claims` image is mostly other people's code — the Quarkus runtime, its transitive libraries, the base operating-system layers — pulled in at build time.
That is normal and good; it is also the attack surface.
When a critical flaw is found in a widely used library (Log4Shell in `log4j-core`, in December 2021, is the canonical example), the hard questions are not about the code you wrote — they are *whether you can prove what is in each image, who built it, and that the one running in production is the one your pipeline shipped.*

A trusted software supply chain answers three questions about every artifact you build:

Provenance:: _Where did this come from?_ Who built it, from which source, with which builder — and can you prove the running image is that exact build, not a substitute.
Integrity:: _Has it been tampered with?_ A cryptographic *signature* that the image you run is byte-for-byte the image your pipeline produced.
Contents:: _What is inside?_ A *software bill of materials* (SBOM) — the itemized list of every component and version, produced at build time and _attestable_ so it travels with the signed image. (Turning that list into a vulnerability answer — "are we affected by the flaw that just broke the internet?" — is *scanning*, the subject of the Application Security Testing module; here the SBOM is the trust artifact that records _what shipped_.)

[TIP]
.SLSA, in one paragraph
====
*SLSA* (Supply-chain Levels for Software Artifacts, pronounced "salsa") is an industry framework that grades _how trustworthy your build process is_ — not your code, your *process*.
The levels climb roughly like this: *build on a hosted system* (not a laptop), *produce signed provenance* (the build system attests what it did), and *make that provenance tamper-resistant and complete*.
You do not need to memorize the level numbers.
What matters is the shape: a pipeline that builds on the cluster, signs what it produces, and attaches machine-readable provenance is *already* climbing SLSA — and that is exactly what Tekton Chains gives you in this module for free.
====

== The signature / attestation / SBOM triangle

Three artifacts travel with a trustworthy image, and they answer three *different* questions.
People conflate them constantly, so pin the distinction now:

[cols="1,3,2",options="header"]
|===
| Artifact | Answers | Produced by (in this lab)

| *Signature* | "This image is intact and came from us." A cryptographic signature over the image digest. | Tekton Chains (cosign key)
| *Attestation (provenance)* | "Here is _how_ this image was built" — the builder, the source, the steps, the timestamps. A signed *SLSA provenance* statement. | Tekton Chains (in-toto / SLSA)
| *SBOM* | "Here is _what is inside_" — every component and version. | The build (CycloneDX)
|===

A signature with no SBOM tells you the image is authentic but not what is in it.
An SBOM with no signature tells you what _a_ build contained but not that _this_ image is that build.
You need all three, and they reinforce each other: the attestation is _signed_, the SBOM can be _attested_, and the signature is what an admission gate _checks_.

== Policy as a gate, and policy as an admission control

There are two places to stop a bad image, and they are not interchangeable — a mature supply chain uses *both*.

Policy-as-gate (build time)::
A gate runs *inside the pipeline*: build the image, check it, and *fail the build* if it violates policy.
Scanning gates — a vulnerable dependency, a hard-coded secret, an insecure manifest — are the subject of the Application Security Testing module (a separate module); the lab here includes one scan step only as the pipeline context that trust attaches to.
The gate's value is *shift-left*: the flaw is caught in the developer's own pipeline run, not in production.

Policy-as-admission (run time)::
A gate only helps if _every_ image goes through the pipeline — and this run-time control is where *this* module lives.
*Admission* closes the gap by enforcing a rule at the cluster: OpenShift's native `ImagePolicy` tells the node to *verify a signature before it will pull an image* into a namespace.
An unsigned image — one that never went through your signing pipeline — is *refused at pull time*.
The gate is a policy you _run_; admission is a policy the platform _enforces_ whether or not anyone remembered to run the gate.
Note this is a *different* check from a scanner's deploy-time policy: the Application Security Testing module gates an insecure _manifest_ with `roxctl deployment check`, while admission here verifies the image's _signature_ — is it provably ours? — not its configuration.

[NOTE]
.The scan in this lab is deliberately minimal
====
The lab's one scan step is scoped to a single enforced policy so it fires *deterministically* on a known-bad dependency — just enough to give the trust controls a realistic pipeline to attach to.
Tuning scan gates so they fire on the right things without crying wolf — and the SAST/SCA/DAST layers that catch what an image scan cannot — is its own craft, and its own module (*Application Security Testing*).
====

== Red Hat trusted content and the base image

The base image is the largest single source of what you ship, and it is the thread from the earlier build module.
`parasol-claims` builds `FROM` a Red Hat *Universal Base Image* (UBI) OpenJDK layer.
For a *trust* story, the point is *provenance*: UBI images are built and signed by Red Hat, so the bottom of your image already has a supply chain you did not have to assemble — a signed foundation you can verify rather than take on faith.
Keeping that base image *current* matters too, but for a different reason: an older tag carries known base-operating-system CVEs a newer tag has fixed.
That currency is a *scanning* concern (the Application Security Testing module), and the lab treats a base-image bump as exactly that — complementary hardening, a good habit, but *not* part of proving the image is yours.

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *When the next big CVE lands, having the receipts is what saves the weekend.*
Before SBOMs, "are we affected?" triggers a fire drill.
After, you have a stored, itemized record of what every image contains — the SBOM this pipeline attaches to each build.
_Querying_ those records at scale to find an affected component is the scanning story (the Application Security Testing module, and Red Hat Trusted Profile Analyzer); the *trust* story is that the record exists, is accurate, and travels with a signed image.
* *Signing is only half the story; enforcement is the other half.*
Plenty of organizations sign their images and then never check the signature at run time.
Admission control (`ImagePolicy` here, or RHACS's cluster-wide admission) is what turns "we sign our images" into "the cluster will not run an unsigned one" — the difference between a policy on a wiki and a policy the platform enforces.
* *Provenance is what an auditor actually asks for.*
"Show me that the image in production came from this commit through this pipeline" is a real audit question.
A signed SLSA attestation is a machine-checkable answer — and because Tekton Chains produces it automatically, you get the answer without anyone writing it up.
* *Trust and scanning are complementary, not the same thing.*
A scanned-clean image that isn't signed can still be swapped for another; a signed image full of known CVEs is authentically vulnerable.
This module makes an image *provably yours*; the Application Security Testing module (a separate module) makes it *provably free of known flaws* — you want both, and they run in the same pipeline.

[NOTE]
.Where this sits — pipelines, scanning, and GitOps
====
The Pipelines Fundamentals module gave you a pipeline that produces an image and *data* (results).
This module adds the *trust* controls to that pipeline — a signature and SLSA provenance on every image, an attestable SBOM, and admission that refuses anything unsigned.
Its sibling, *Application Security Testing* (a separate module), adds the *scanning* controls — SAST, SCA, DAST, and image-risk gates that prove the image is free of known flaws.
The GitOps modules that follow take the *trustworthy, signed* image and reconcile it into each environment — so admission control (only-signed-runs) and GitOps promotion are the two halves of "the right image, and only the right image, reaches production" (all separate modules).
====
