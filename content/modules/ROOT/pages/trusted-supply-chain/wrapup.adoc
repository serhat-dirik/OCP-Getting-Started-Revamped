The idea under all of it: *trust is three facts about an artifact — what is inside it, who built it, and whether it is intact — produced automatically, recorded where anyone can audit them, and enforced by the platform*, so "prove this image is safe to run" is a query and a policy, not a promise.

== Map to your org

. *Do your builds produce and keep a bill of materials at all?*
You generated an SBOM and read what shipped in one line.
For a real service in your org: is there a stored, attestable record of what every image contains — one that travels with the signed image — or would "what's in this image?" mean a team grepping build files by hand?
(Querying those records across the estate when a CVE lands is the scanning module's job; this prompt is about whether the record _exists_ in the first place.)
. *Do you sign your images — and does anything check the signature?*
Signing without verification is theater.
If your org signs images today, where is the signature actually _enforced_ — at admission on every cluster, or nowhere?
If it does not sign yet, the Tekton Chains path you saw needs no per-build work — what stops you turning it on?
. *Where should the gate live — the pipeline, admission, or both?*
You saw a build-time scan gate and a run-time admission control.
For a service that matters, which failures do you want caught in a developer's pipeline run (shift-left) versus refused at the cluster no matter what?
Most mature answers are "both" — which one is missing in your pipeline today?

== When *not* to reach for this

The controls in this module are the right default for anything you ship — which means being honest about their edges:

* *Don't switch on signature admission without scoping and a break-glass path.*
An `ImagePolicy` that demands _your_ signature will also refuse *legitimate* images that were never signed with your key — a third-party base image, a vendor's operator, an emergency hotfix built off-pipeline.
Scope admission to the repositories you actually sign, decide who can grant a documented, time-boxed exception, and roll it out warn-before-enforce — otherwise the first unsigned-but-needed image turns a security control into an outage.
* *Don't sign images you never verify.*
If no admission control or downstream check ever validates the signature, signing is pure overhead.
Turn signing on _with_ a place that enforces it — otherwise you have added a step that produces an artifact nobody reads.
* *Keyless signing is powerful, but it is not free.*
The keyless flow (Fulcio short-lived certs + a Rekor transparency log) removes long-lived keys, but it adds an OpenID Connect (OIDC) identity dependency, a transparency-log retention question, and more moving parts.
For a controlled build system, a well-managed cosign key is often the simpler, sufficient choice — reach for keyless when you need public verifiability or per-identity signing, not by default.
* *An SBOM is a snapshot, not a subscription.*
The bill of materials is accurate the moment it is built; it does not tell you a component became vulnerable _after_ the build.
Storing SBOMs is what lets you re-query them when a _new_ CVE lands — the SBOM is only as useful as the process that keeps and re-scans it.

== Go deeper

* *Application Security Testing* — the *scanning* half of DevSecOps and this module's closest sibling: SAST, SCA, DAST, configuration, and image-risk gates that prove an image is *free of known flaws*, where this module proves it is *provably yours*. You want both, running in the same pipeline (a separate module).
* *GitOps Fundamentals* — this module made the image _trustworthy_ and taught the cluster to refuse an _unsigned_ one; the GitOps module is where that signed image is reconciled into each environment from Git, so "only the right image reaches production" is the two halves together (a separate module).
* *Registry, Images & Catalog Governance* — the cluster-wide version of the admission control you saw: allowed-registries policy, mirroring, and where images may come from (a separate module).
* *Red Hat Trusted Profile Analyzer* (RHTPA) — the enterprise "SBOM at scale" story: ingest every build's SBOM and query your whole estate for an affected component from one place (the RHADS component this lab's terminal `jq` foreshadows).
* Red Hat documentation: *Advanced Cluster Security for Kubernetes* (RHACS) "Verifying image signatures" and build-time policy; *OpenShift Pipelines* "Using Tekton Chains for supply chain security"; *Trusted Artifact Signer* (RHTAS); and OpenShift "Managing secure signatures with sigstore" (`ImagePolicy`).
