The one idea under all of it: you never operated a Pod directly.
You declared *desired state* on a Deployment and let the reconcile loop do the operating — scaling, replacing, and load-balancing on your behalf.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams run software today:

. *Who runs your clusters, and what do you self-serve versus file a ticket for?*
On OpenShift, deploying, scaling, and exposing an app were all self-service in your own project.
Which of those are ticket-and-wait in your organization right now — and what would change if they were not?
. *What dies when a VM dies today?*
You killed a Pod and the platform replaced it in seconds with no page.
Pick one real production service: if the box it runs on vanished tonight, how long until it is back, and who has to be awake for it?
. *Where are your pets?*
Name three "servers with names" your organization still hand-nurses.
For each, is it a pet because the *workload* genuinely needs to be (stateful, licensed, legacy) — or only because nobody has made it cattle yet?

== When *not* to do it this way

Self-healing, disposable workloads are the right default — but "the right default" is not "always".
Be honest about the edges:

* *Don't put a pet on the platform and keep treating it like a pet.*
If you deploy something but still SSH in to hand-edit it, restart it manually, or store its only copy of important data inside the Pod, you have all the constraints of the platform and none of the benefit.
Either make it cattle (externalize its state and config — the next modules show how) or leave it where it is.
* *A VM is still the right answer sometimes.*
Not every workload should be a container: a legacy appliance, a kernel-module-heavy application, or a vendor-supported VM image may be genuinely better run as a virtual machine.
OpenShift can run those too — as first-class VMs beside your containers — which is a later module (OpenShift Virtualization for App Teams).
The skill is choosing, not forcing everything into a Pod.
* *Stateful data needs more than a Deployment.*
Your claims portal today is stateless — killing a Pod costs nothing.
A database is not: replace its Pod and you can lose data unless storage and identity are handled properly.
That is a deliberately separate module (Storage & Stateful Apps); do not run a production database as a naive Deployment.
* *Scale-to-zero and request-driven compute are their own tool.*
You scaled by hand today.
Some workloads should scale automatically, even to zero between requests — that is Serverless, not a fixed-replica Deployment.
Different job, different module.
