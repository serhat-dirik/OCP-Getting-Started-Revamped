[.lead]
The shiny developer catalog in the Build & Deliver module — the builder images, the one-click samples, the operators — did not appear by magic: a platform team *curated* every entry, and decided which registries an image may even come from.
This module is the layer *underneath* that catalog: how the internal registry stores your images, why an ImageStream is still worth using, and how platform engineers govern *where images come from* and *what developers can run and offer*.
Same Parasol claims image, same cluster — but by the end you can promote it by immutable digest, re-import a base image on a schedule, deploy it with a private-registry pull secret, publish your own catalog entry, and read the cluster-wide controls that decide the rest.

== What you'll learn

* How the *internal registry* is wired on this cluster — an in-cluster Service, backed by one volume, with a nightly *pruner* — and why it has no external route here.
* Why an *ImageStream* still earns its place: it pins a moving tag to an *immutable digest*, gives you a stable in-cluster name, and can *re-import* an external image on a schedule.
* How to *tag and promote* an image across environments (`1.0` → `prod`) so a promotion is a pointer move, not a rebuild — and why the promoted tag resolves to the *exact same bits*.
* How to pull from an *external or private registry* with a *pull secret*, linked to a ServiceAccount or named on a pod — the mechanics that let a workload authenticate to a registry it doesn't own.
* The *trust boundary*: where images may come from — *allowed registries*, *import restrictions*, and *IDMS/ITMS mirroring* — and how it complements the *signature* trust from the Trusted Supply Chain module.
* How platform teams *feed the catalog*: OperatorHub sources, the *samples operator*, cluster *ConsoleSamples*, your own namespaced *Templates*, and where devfiles come from.

== Two supply chains on one page

Everything in this module is one of *two* supply chains that meet at the developer.
The *image supply* decides which bits run; the *catalog supply* decides what a developer can pick from.
A platform team governs both.

== The internal registry, honestly

Every OpenShift cluster ships an *integrated container registry*.
On this cluster it lives at `image-registry.openshift-image-registry.svc:5000` — an in-cluster Service, reachable only from *inside* the cluster.
It is where your builds push and where the platform caches images it pulls through on your behalf.

Two facts about *this* cluster shape the whole lab, and both are deliberate:

* *There is no external route.* The registry's `defaultRoute` is off, so you cannot `podman login` and push to it from your laptop.
Everything you do with images here runs *through the API* — `oc tag`, `oc import-image`, in-cluster builds — never a `docker push`.
That's the common posture for a shared workshop or a locked-down production cluster: the registry is an *internal* service, not an internet-facing one.
* *Storage is one volume, and a pruner runs nightly.* The registry is backed by a single read-write-once PVC with a `Recreate` rollout (one registry pod at a time), and an *image pruner* runs on a schedule — on this cluster daily at midnight, keeping the last 3 tag revisions and sparing anything younger than an hour.
On a multi-day event that matters: an unreferenced image can be *pruned overnight*. Keep what you need *referenced* — by a running workload or a tag — and it stays.

[NOTE]
.Quay is the registry you'd reach for at scale
====
The integrated registry is perfect for in-cluster builds and caching.
When an organization needs a *shared*, multi-cluster registry with vulnerability scanning, geo-replication, and fine-grained access control, that's *Red Hat Quay* — a separate product, mentioned here so you know where the internal registry stops and a full enterprise registry begins.
This module uses the *internal* registry throughout.
====

== Why an ImageStream still matters

A container image reference like `parasol-claims:prod` is a *moving target* — whoever pushes that tag next changes what it points to.
An *ImageStream* is OpenShift's answer: a stable, in-cluster name that *tracks* image tags and *records the immutable digest* behind each one.

Three properties earn it a place in your workflow:

* *It pins a tag to a digest.* When you tag or import, the ImageStream records the `sha256:…` digest the tag resolved to. A digest is *content-addressed* — it can never point at different bits. So "promote `1.0` to `prod`" becomes a pointer to an *exact image*, not a hope that nobody re-pushed the tag.
* *It can re-import on a schedule.* Mark a tag `scheduled` and OpenShift periodically re-checks the source registry and pulls the new digest when the upstream tag moves — a base image that stays current without a human watching it.
* *`referencePolicy: Local` turns it into a pull-through cache.* With Local reference, workloads pull the image *from the internal registry* rather than the source. The registry fetches and caches the bits once; every pod after that pulls locally — faster, and resilient to the source being briefly unreachable.

That last one is how the seeded `parasol-claims` stream works in the lab: its `1.0` tag references a shared build via `referencePolicy: Local`, so the internal registry serves the bits and no per-pod pull secret is needed.
ImageStreams also drive *triggers* — a Deployment can watch a stream and roll automatically when the tag's digest changes — the mechanism behind "rebuild pushes, app redeploys" in the build modules.

== The trust boundary: where images may come from

Two different questions decide whether an image is allowed to run, and they're *complementary*:

* *Is this image trusted?* — is it *signed* by someone you trust? That's the *sigstore signature admission* you met in the Trusted Supply Chain module (`ImagePolicy` / `ClusterImagePolicy`). A *different* axis, not repeated here.
* *May images come from this registry at all?* — that's *this* module's boundary, and it's set cluster-wide on the `Image` config object:

[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Control | What it governs | Blast radius

| `allowedRegistriesForImport` | Which registries a *normal user* may `oc import-image` / tag from | *Instant*, admission-level — no node change; blocks *imports*, not running pods
| `registrySources.allowedRegistries` / `blockedRegistries` | Which registries *any pod or build* may pull/push, enforced by the container runtime on *every* node | *Heavy* — the Machine Config Operator re-renders every node and *rolls the pool*
| *IDMS / ITMS* (ImageDigestMirrorSet / ImageTagMirrorSet) | Redirect pulls of a registry to a *mirror* — the heart of *disconnected* installs | *Heavy* — also re-rendered onto every node
|===

*IDMS* and *ITMS* are the *current* mirroring APIs — an IDMS redirects pulls *by digest*, an ITMS *by tag*, to a mirror registry inside your network.
This is how a disconnected (air-gapped) cluster runs: every image reference is transparently mirror-served.
(An older object, `ImageContentSourcePolicy`, did this before OpenShift 4.13 and is *deprecated* — you convert it to IDMS with `oc adm migrate icsp`; it is not a path you'd choose today.)

The honest operational note, and why the lab *reads* these rather than *sets* them: changing `registrySources` or applying an IDMS/ITMS re-renders `/etc/containers/registries.conf` on *every* node and *reboots the pool* — minutes of churn that reschedules every tenant's pods.
`allowedRegistriesForImport`, by contrast, is admission-only and *instant* — which is why it's the one control your instructor can safely toggle live.

== The catalog supply: what developers can run and offer

The Developer Catalog you browsed in the Build & Deliver module is *fed* from several sources, each a governance lever a platform team pulls:

* *OperatorHub sources & `CatalogSource`s* — the operators offered in OperatorHub come from catalog sources. The default four (Red Hat, Certified, Community, Marketplace) can be trimmed on the `OperatorHub` config, and an organization can *add* its own `CatalogSource` — this cluster has one, a curated internal catalog alongside the defaults.
* *The samples operator* — the builder images and quickstart templates in the catalog (60 image streams and 51 templates on this cluster) are managed by one operator. A platform team can *skip* an individual sample it doesn't want offered, using `skippedImagestreams` / `skippedTemplates` — a scalpel, never the all-or-nothing `managementState: Removed`, which would delete *every* sample.
* *`ConsoleSample`* — a cluster-scoped object that adds a *sample* to the catalog (from a Git import or a container image) — how an organization publishes "here's our blessed starting point."
* *Namespaced `Template`s* — the one catalog lever *you* control. A `Template` you create in your *own* project appears in *your* Developer Catalog and nobody else's — a self-service catalog entry with no cluster-admin needed. You'll publish one in the lab.
* *Devfiles* — the "Import from Git" devfile samples come from a devfile registry (the community `registry.devfile.io`, plus any private one). A *private* devfile registry is a Dev Spaces (CheCluster) configuration — the same idea, governed in the workspaces module — so here it's a *peek*, not a cluster API you set.

The through-line: *the platform team decides what developers can run and offer.* Most of that is cluster-wide and governed centrally; the one piece you own is a Template in your own namespace.

== Who runs what

Because so much of this surface is a *cluster singleton*, the module splits cleanly:

* *Hands-on, in your own namespace (non-disruptive):* tour the registry and the `parasol-claims` stream, tag/promote, scheduled import, link a pull secret, publish a namespaced Template, and *read* the governance objects.
* *Instructor demo (cluster-wide):* blocking imports with `allowedRegistriesForImport` (instant, live), `registrySources` + IDMS/ITMS mirroring (talk-through — they reboot nodes), disabling a stock sample, and adding a `ConsoleSample`. You'll *read* the results of these; *setting* them affects every tenant.

You read the cluster-wide objects through the read-only `platform-observer` role — the same pattern as the other platform modules.

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Which promotion is running in prod — and is it the image we tested?"* If "promote to prod" means re-pushing a tag, you're trusting that nobody changed it in between. Promoting by *digest* through an ImageStream makes the answer provable: `prod` and the tested `1.0` resolve to the *same* `sha256`, or they don't. That's the difference between a release you can audit and one you hope about.
* *"A developer pulled a random image off the internet into production."* Without an allowed-registries policy, any `oc import-image docker.io/whatever` succeeds. `allowedRegistriesForImport` is the instant, low-risk floor; `registrySources` and mirroring are the heavier, disconnected-grade controls. Most breaches of this kind are *policy not set*, not policy defeated.
* *"Our air-gapped cluster can't reach the internet — how does anything pull?"* Every image is mirror-served through IDMS/ITMS to an internal registry. Teams that haven't set this up discover it the hard way during their first disconnected install; the mechanism is worth understanding *before* you need it.
* *"Why is our catalog full of things we don't allow, and missing the one blessed starter we do?"* The catalog is *curated*, not fixed. Skipping the samples you don't want and publishing a `ConsoleSample` (or a namespaced Template) for the one you do is a half-hour of platform work that saves every developer the guesswork.
