== What you built

You took Parasol's claims chain from *three naked services* to a *governed mesh*.
You *enrolled* `parasol-web`, `parasol-claims`, and `parasol-fraud` per-workload and watched the native-sidecar proxy appear beside each app ‚Äî while the database and the test client stayed out.
You proved *mutual TLS* is real by flipping a `PeerAuthentication` to STRICT and watching a plaintext client get *refused*, and you read a workload's *SPIFFE identity* (`sa/parasol-claims`) straight off its certificate.
You made the traffic *visible* in Kiali, then *shiftable* ‚Äî a 90/10 weighted `VirtualService` with a header route to a new fraud version, measured from the mesh's own telemetry.
You added *resilience* ‚Äî a fault, a timeout, retries, and a circuit breaker ‚Äî and learned the honest boundary of each.
You *secured* the call path so that *only* `sa/parasol-claims` may call fraud, with a live `403` for everyone else.
And in the encore you served a *raw-TCP* partner feed through an Istio ingress *Gateway* and *rate-limited* it with an `EnvoyFilter` ‚Äî the ingress paths Routes can't take.

The one idea under all of it: *a mesh turns cross-service concerns into platform policy* ‚Äî encryption, observability, traffic control, and access are *configuration istiod pushes to proxies*, not code every team rewrites, and every one of them is bound to *workload identity* rather than network location.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your services talk to each other today:

. *Is your east-west traffic provably encrypted ‚Äî or just assumed safe because it's "internal"?*
You flipped STRICT and watched a plaintext caller get refused. Could you make the same statement about your production namespaces ‚Äî that service-to-service traffic is mutual-TLS encrypted and unauthenticated callers are *rejected* ‚Äî and prove it in an audit, or is "it's inside the cluster" the whole answer today?
. *When a dependency gets slow, does it fail fast ‚Äî or take the caller down with it?*
You watched a 5-second hang land on the caller and configured timeouts, retries, and a circuit breaker. Do your services have those *anywhere* ‚Äî in code, inconsistently, per team ‚Äî or would one slow downstream still pile up threads until the whole path falls over? A mesh makes it uniform platform policy.
. *Can you canary a new version to 10% of traffic without a redeploy?*
You shifted 90/10 with one `VirtualService` and pinned test users with a header. Is that a one-resource change in your environment, or a load-balancer ticket and a second-Deployment dance every time?
. *Who is allowed to call your most sensitive service ‚Äî and can you prove it by identity?*
"Only claims may call fraud" was enforced by SPIFFE identity, not IP. For your equivalent of the fraud service ‚Äî the one that moves money or touches PII ‚Äî is access controlled by *who the workload is*, or just by *where it sits* on the network?
. *When a partner needs raw TCP or TLS passthrough, do you have an answer?*
You served a raw-TCP feed through an Istio Gateway where a Route couldn't. When a partner integration needs a non-HTTP protocol or SNI passthrough, is that a known, supported path in your platform ‚Äî or a scramble?

== When *not* to reach for this

A mesh is powerful, and over-reaching with it is its own failure mode:

* *Don't mesh a single app with no east-west calls.*
If a service just receives HTTP from a Route and talks to a database, a mesh adds a proxy per pod and a control plane to run for *nothing* it needs ‚Äî a Route, a NetworkPolicy, and a metrics endpoint already cover it. The mesh earns its cost when *many services call each other* and you need identity, traffic control, and uniform observability *across* them.
* *Don't use an `AuthorizationPolicy` where a NetworkPolicy is the right tool.*
Coarse "namespace A can't reach namespace B" is a *NetworkPolicy* ‚Äî cheaper, enforced at the network layer, no mesh required. Reserve `AuthorizationPolicy` for *identity-based* L7 rules ("only this workload, on this path, with this method"). Use both as layers; don't make the mesh do the network layer's job.
* *Don't reach for an `EnvoyFilter` when a first-class resource exists.*
`EnvoyFilter` patches the proxy's internal config directly ‚Äî powerful, but version-fragile and easy to break on upgrade. If a `VirtualService`, `DestinationRule`, or `Gateway` field does what you need, use it. Save `EnvoyFilter` for genuine gaps (the local rate limit in the encore), and treat each one as tech debt to re-verify on every mesh upgrade.
* *Don't treat a gateway rate limit as API management.*
The `EnvoyFilter` local rate limit is per-proxy, coarse protection ‚Äî great for shielding a backend from a flood. Per-consumer quotas, plans, keys, and analytics are a *different* product (a full API-management platform). Framing a gateway rate limit as "we have API management" will disappoint the first team that needs a per-customer quota.
* *Don't keep mutual TLS at PERMISSIVE forever.*
PERMISSIVE is the *on-ramp* ‚Äî it lets you enroll without breaking un-meshed callers. Leaving it there permanently means plaintext is still accepted, so the encryption you "have" isn't actually *enforced*. The production target is STRICT, with external traffic entering through an ingress gateway; PERMISSIVE is a migration state, not a destination.

== Go deeper

* *OpenShift Service Mesh 3 ‚Äî architecture and the Sail operator* ‚Äî how upstream Istio is packaged, the `Istio`/`IstioCNI` control-plane resources, and the 2.x‚Üí3.x migration path (the control-plane and member-roll resources are gone; it's upstream Istio now).
* *Sidecar and ambient data planes* ‚Äî the injection model you used, the native-sidecar init container, and *ambient* mode (per-node ztunnel + optional waypoint) as the lower-overhead direction for large fleets.
* *Traffic management* ‚Äî `VirtualService` and `DestinationRule` in depth: weighted routing, header/URI matching, mirroring (dark launch), timeouts, retries, and outlier detection (the circuit breaker).
* *Security* ‚Äî `PeerAuthentication` (mesh-wide and per-workload mutual TLS modes), `AuthorizationPolicy` (ALLOW/DENY, source principals, operations), and the SPIFFE identity model behind them.
* *Observability* ‚Äî Kiali (the graph, health, and validation), and wiring mesh metrics and *distributed tracing* (Tempo/OpenTelemetry) so a request is one trace across the mesh ‚Äî the tie-in to the observability module.
* *Ingress gateways and the L4 boundary* ‚Äî the Istio `Gateway` for HTTP(S), raw-TCP, and TLS passthrough; the current status of the experimental L4 Gateway API resources; and `EnvoyFilter` as the low-level escape hatch, used sparingly.
