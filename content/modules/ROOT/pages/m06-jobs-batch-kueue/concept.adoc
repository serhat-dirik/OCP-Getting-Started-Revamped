[.lead]
Parasol runs its claims work in two speeds: the API that answers a customer in milliseconds, and the *batch* that grinds through every open claim overnight ‚Äî statements, fraud scoring, actuarial rollups.
The overnight work has quietly grown until one team's month-end run can starve everyone else's jobs off the cluster.
This module is about running that batch tier *well* on the platform: Jobs and CronJobs for the work itself, and admission control so no single team's batch can crowd out the rest ‚Äî including the newest batch of all, AI inference.

== What you'll learn

* Where *batch* sits on the async spectrum next to request-driven and event-driven work, and when a Job is the right tool.
* How the *Job controller* actually behaves ‚Äî `completions`, `parallelism`, `backoffLimit`, `activeDeadlineSeconds`, and *Indexed* Jobs that shard a dataset.
* How to schedule recurring work with a *CronJob* (`concurrencyPolicy`, `startingDeadlineSeconds`, `suspend`) without piling runs on top of each other.
* Why org-scale batch needs *admission control*, and how *Red Hat build of Kueue* adds quotas, priorities, and preemption on top of plain Jobs.
* Why *AI batch is just batch* ‚Äî an inference or evaluation Job is admitted, queued, and preempted by exactly the same machinery, and GPU quotas are governed the same way.

== The async spectrum: request, event, batch

Not all work is a web request.
It helps to place workloads on a spectrum by *what triggers them* and *how you wait for the result*:

Request-driven::
A caller is waiting *right now*.
A Deployment behind a Service answers in milliseconds and holds a connection open.
Latency is the metric; you scale replicas to keep it low.
(That is the world of the first modules.)
Event-driven::
Something *happened* ‚Äî a message on a queue, a file landed, a webhook fired ‚Äî and a handler reacts, usually within seconds.
No human is holding a connection, but the work is still small and per-event.
Batch::
A *bounded* body of work runs to completion and then stops ‚Äî "process all 40,000 open claims," "score last night's transactions," "roll up the quarter."
Nobody waits on a socket; you care that it *finishes*, correctly and within a window, not that it answers in 50&nbsp;ms.

The platform has a purpose-built object for the batch end of that spectrum: the *Job*.

== The Job controller, honestly

A *Job* creates one or more Pods and keeps them running until a set number succeed ‚Äî then it stops.
That "then it stops" is the whole difference from a Deployment, which exists to keep Pods running forever.
Four fields do most of the work, and getting them right is the lesson:

`completions`::
How many Pods must *succeed* for the Job to be done.
`completions: 6` means six successful runs.
`parallelism`::
How many Pods may run *at once*.
With `completions: 6, parallelism: 3`, the Job runs three Pods, waits for them, then runs three more ‚Äî two waves.
`backoffLimit`::
How many times a failed Pod is *retried* before the Job gives up and is marked `Failed`.
Retries are *exponentially* backed off (roughly 10&nbsp;s, then 20&nbsp;s, then 40&nbsp;s‚Ä¶), so a crash-looping Job fails slowly, on purpose.
`activeDeadlineSeconds`::
A wall-clock cap on the whole Job ‚Äî a runaway is killed even if it never crashes.

Two shapes of Job matter for real data work:

* An *Indexed* Job (`completionMode: Indexed`) gives each Pod a fixed index, `0 ‚Ä¶ completions-1`, in the `$JOB_COMPLETION_INDEX` environment variable.
That index is how you *shard*: Pod 3 processes shard 3 of the dataset, deterministically, with no coordinator.
It is map/reduce's "map" step with nothing but the Job controller.
* A *CronJob* wraps a Job template on a schedule (standard cron syntax).
It is how "nightly batch" actually runs.
Three fields keep it sane:
`concurrencyPolicy: Forbid` refuses to start a run while the previous one is still going (no overlap);
`startingDeadlineSeconds` says "if you missed the tick by more than this, skip it ‚Äî don't pile up";
`suspend: true` pauses scheduling entirely without deleting anything.

One honest caveat you will feel in the lab: *a Job's Pod template is immutable*.
You cannot edit the command of a running Job ‚Äî you delete it and re-create it.
That is why batch belongs in Git and in pipelines, not in hand-edits.

== Why org-scale batch needs admission control

Plain Jobs have one glaring gap at organization scale: *nothing arbitrates between them*.
Every Job the scheduler can place, it runs.
A namespace `ResourceQuota` caps one team, but it cannot express "the actuarial month-end run may use the cluster's spare capacity, but if the fraud team submits an urgent scoring job, it goes first."
Left alone, the biggest, greediest batch wins, and everyone else waits ‚Äî the *starvation* problem.

*Red Hat build of Kueue* fills that gap.
It sits *above* the Job controller as an *admission* layer: a Job opts in with a label, and Kueue decides *when* it may start ‚Äî holding it *suspended* until there is quota, then releasing it.
Four objects express the policy:

ResourceFlavor::
A class of capacity ("this kind of node / these resources").
One flavor covers the whole cluster here.
ClusterQueue::
Where the *quota* lives ‚Äî "this queue may run 500m CPU and 512Mi of memory of admitted work at a time."
Cluster-scoped, owned by the platform team.
In this workshop *each user gets their own* ClusterQueue, so one attendee's batch cannot starve another's.
LocalQueue::
A namespaced pointer from your project to a ClusterQueue.
It holds *no* quota; it is how your Jobs reach the cluster queue.
A Job joins it with the label `kueue.x-k8s.io/queue-name`.
WorkloadPriorityClass::
A priority number.
A higher-priority Job can *preempt* a lower-priority one ‚Äî evict it back into the queue ‚Äî when the quota is full.

When you submit a labeled Job, Kueue creates a *Workload* object that tracks it through admission: `Pending` ‚Üí `QuotaReserved` ‚Üí `Admitted` (or, if a higher-priority Workload needs the room, `Evicted`/`Preempted` and back to `Pending`).
Reading Workloads is how you *see* the queue working.

The sizing in this workshop is deliberately tiny ‚Äî a ClusterQueue that fits about *two* sample Pods ‚Äî so the queue is *visible*: submit five jobs and you watch two admitted and three pending; submit one high-priority job and you watch it *preempt* a running low-priority one.
On a real cluster the numbers are bigger, but the mechanism is identical.

== The AI angle: batch inference is this decade's nightly report

Here is the payoff.
A *batch inference* job ‚Äî score every claim for fraud risk overnight, run an evaluation suite against a new model, generate embeddings for a document set ‚Äî is *a Job*.
It reads its input, calls a model, writes its output, and stops.
It carries the same `kueue.x-k8s.io/queue-name` label, becomes the same Workload, and is admitted, queued, and preempted by the same ClusterQueue as the statement run.

That is not a coincidence; it is the point.
The industry is converging on Kueue precisely because *GPU* capacity is scarce and expensive, and the same quota-and-priority machinery that keeps a claims batch fair is what keeps a fleet of teams from fighting over GPUs.
A ResourceFlavor can describe GPU nodes; a ClusterQueue can hold `nvidia.com/gpu` quota; a high-priority eval job can preempt a low-priority training run.
Everything you learn here on tiny CPU Jobs is the same control plane that governs AI batch at scale ‚Äî which is why distributed-training and model-evaluation stacks on OpenShift are built on Kueue.

In the lab you run a fraud-scoring inference Job against a *Models-as-a-Service (MaaS)* endpoint, through your LocalQueue, and watch it queue exactly like the statement run.
*AI batch is just batch.*

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Who owns the batch quota?"* is a real org question with a real owner.
The ClusterQueue is the platform team's lever: it decides how much of the cluster the batch tier may use and how teams share it.
When that lever exists, "the month-end run took down the cluster" stops being a quarterly incident.
* *Cron sprawl is technical debt.*
Teams accumulate dozens of CronJobs nobody owns, half of them overlapping, some that should have been event-driven the day a queue became available.
A CronJob you can't name the owner of is a 2&nbsp;a.m. page waiting to happen.
* *AI workloads will force the conversation whether you plan for it or not.*
The first time two teams want the same GPUs at the same time, someone invents an admission policy ‚Äî usually a spreadsheet and a Slack argument.
Kueue is that policy as code, and adopting it *before* the GPU crunch is far cheaper than during it.
