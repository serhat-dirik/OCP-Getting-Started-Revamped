[.lead]
Parasol runs its claims work at two speeds: an API that answers a customer in milliseconds, and a *batch* tier that grinds through every open claim overnight — statements, fraud scoring, actuarial rollups.
That overnight work has quietly grown until one team's month-end run can starve everyone else off the cluster.
This module is about running batch *well*: Jobs and CronJobs for the work itself, plus admission control so no single team can crowd out the rest — including the newest kind of batch, AI inference.

== What you'll learn

* What *batch* work is, how it differs from a web request, and when a *Job* is the right tool for it.
* How a *Job* runs one-off work to completion — and the few fields (`completions`, `parallelism`, `backoffLimit`) that steer it.
* How an *Indexed* Job splits a dataset so each Pod processes its own slice, with no central coordinator.
* How a *CronJob* runs batch on a schedule without letting slow runs pile up on each other.
* Why sharing a cluster fairly needs *admission control*, and how *Red Hat build of Kueue* adds quotas, priorities, and preemption on top of plain Jobs.
* Why *AI batch is just batch* — an inference Job is queued and preempted by exactly the same machinery, which is how teams end up sharing scarce GPUs.

== The async spectrum: request, event, batch

Not all work is a web request.
A useful way to sort workloads is by two questions: *what triggers them*, and *how you wait for the result*.

Request-driven::
A caller is waiting *right now*.
A Deployment behind a Service answers in milliseconds and holds the connection open.
Latency is what you optimize; you add replicas to keep it low.
This is the world of the earlier modules.
Event-driven::
Something *happened* — a message arrived, a file landed, a webhook fired — and a handler reacts, usually within seconds.
No human waits on a connection, but the work is still small and per-event.
Batch::
A *bounded* pile of work runs start to finish, then stops: "process all 40,000 open claims", "score last night's transactions", "roll up the quarter".
Nobody waits on a socket.
What matters is that it *finishes* — correctly, and inside its window — not that it replies in 50&nbsp;ms.

The platform has a purpose-built object for the batch end of that spectrum: the *Job*.

== A Job runs to completion, then stops

A *Deployment* keeps Pods running forever — kill one and it is replaced.
A *Job* is the opposite: it starts one or more Pods, waits until a set number of them *succeed*, and then it stops.
That "then it stops" is the whole idea of batch.
The work is finite, and finishing it is the goal.

Four fields do most of the steering, and getting them right is the lesson:

`completions`::
How many Pods must *succeed* before the Job is done.
`completions: 6` means six successful runs.
`parallelism`::
How many Pods may run *at the same time*.
With `completions: 6` and `parallelism: 3`, the Job runs three Pods, waits for them, then runs three more — two waves.
`backoffLimit`::
How many times a failed Pod is *retried* before the Job gives up and is marked `Failed`.
Retries back off *exponentially* (roughly 10&nbsp;s, then 20&nbsp;s, then 40&nbsp;s), so a crash-looping Job fails slowly, on purpose.
`activeDeadlineSeconds`::
A wall-clock time limit on the whole Job.
A runaway is killed even if it never actually crashes.

One caveat you will feel in the lab: *a Job's Pod template is immutable*.
You cannot edit the command of a Job that already exists — you delete it and create it again.
That sounds annoying, but it is the platform pushing batch definitions into files and Git, where they belong, and out of live hand-edits.

== Indexed Jobs: split the data, no coordinator

`parallelism` runs several Pods at once, but by default every Pod runs the *same* command.
That is fine when each Pod does identical work — and useless when you want to *split a dataset* so each Pod handles a different part.

An *Indexed* Job (`completionMode: Indexed`) is how you split it.
Kubernetes gives each Pod a number — its *index*, from `0` to `completions - 1` — in the environment variable `$JOB_COMPLETION_INDEX`.
Your script reads that number and works out which slice of the data is its own.

Here is the whole idea in one example.
Say you have a 10,000-row file and you set `completions: 10`.
Pod 0 takes rows 0–999, Pod 3 takes rows 3000–3999, Pod 9 takes the last thousand.
Nobody hands out those assignments — *the index is the assignment*.
Each Pod works out its own slice from its own number and never has to talk to the others.

That last part is the point: there is *no coordinator*.
No manager Pod divides the work and deals it out.
Picture a mailroom with numbered pigeonholes: the clerk on slot 3 handles everything for slot 3, and knows it is theirs from the number alone — no supervisor stands at the door dealing out letters.
Hand ten clerks their numbers and the mail sorts itself, all at once.

This is exactly the *map* step of map/reduce, built from nothing but the Job controller: partition the data by index, then process each part on its own.
In the lab you will watch five Pods each take a clean fifth of the claims — their Pod names even carry the index, `...-0` through `...-4`.

== CronJobs: batch on a schedule

Most "nightly batch" is not launched by hand — it runs on a clock.
A *CronJob* wraps a Job template in standard cron syntax (`0 2 * * *` reads as "02:00 every day") and creates a fresh Job on each tick.

Three fields keep a schedule out of trouble:

`concurrencyPolicy: Forbid`::
Do not start a new run while the previous one is still going — no overlap.
`startingDeadlineSeconds`::
If a tick is missed by more than this many seconds, skip it rather than build a backlog.
`suspend: true`::
Pause the schedule without deleting anything; flip it back to resume.

Everything you learned about Jobs still applies — each run *is* a Job, with its own `completions`, `backoffLimit`, and immutable template.
A CronJob just decides *when* they start.

== Why batch needs a referee: admission control

Plain Jobs share a cluster badly.
Nothing *arbitrates between them*: every Job the scheduler can place, it runs.
A namespace `ResourceQuota` can cap one team's total, but it cannot say "the actuarial month-end run may use spare capacity — but if the fraud team submits an urgent job, that goes first".
Left alone, the biggest, greediest batch wins and everyone else waits.
That is the *starvation* problem, and at organization scale it is a recurring incident, not a hypothetical.

Think of a busy restaurant with more parties than tables.
With no host, the pushiest crowd grabs every table and everyone else stands at the door.
A *maître d'* fixes that: seat parties as tables free up, keep a waiting list, and — when a VIP reservation arrives to a full room — ask one walk-in party to give up its table.
*Red Hat build of Kueue* is that maître d' for batch.

It sits *above* the Job controller as an *admission* layer.
A Job opts in with a label; Kueue then decides *when* it may start, holding it *suspended* until there is room and releasing it once there is.
Four objects express the policy:

ResourceFlavor::
A class of capacity ("this kind of node, these resources").
One flavor covers the whole cluster in this workshop.
ClusterQueue::
Where the *quota* lives — "this queue may run 500m CPU and 512Mi of admitted work at a time".
It is cluster-scoped and owned by the platform team.
In this workshop *each user gets their own* ClusterQueue, so one attendee's batch cannot starve another's.
LocalQueue::
A namespaced pointer from your project to a ClusterQueue.
It holds *no* quota of its own; it is simply how your Jobs reach the cluster queue.
A Job joins it with the label `kueue.x-k8s.io/queue-name`.
WorkloadPriorityClass::
A priority number.
When the quota is full, a higher-priority Job can *preempt* a lower-priority one — evict it back into the queue and take its place.

Behind the scenes, Kueue tracks each labeled Job with a *Workload* object as it moves through admission: `Pending` → `QuotaReserved` → `Admitted` (or, when a higher-priority Workload needs the room, `Evicted`/`Preempted` and back to `Pending`).
Reading Workloads is how you *watch the queue work*.

The quota in this workshop is deliberately tiny — a ClusterQueue that fits about *two* sample Pods — so the queue is easy to *see*.
Submit five jobs and you watch two get admitted and three sit pending.
Submit one high-priority job and you watch it *preempt* a running low-priority one.
On a real cluster the numbers are bigger; the mechanism is identical.

== AI batch is just batch

Here is the payoff.
A *batch-inference* job — score every claim for fraud risk overnight, run an evaluation suite against a new model, generate embeddings for a set of documents — is *a Job*.
It reads its input, calls a model, writes its output, and stops.
It carries the same `kueue.x-k8s.io/queue-name` label, becomes the same Workload, and is admitted, queued, and preempted by the same ClusterQueue as the statement run.

That is not a coincidence — it is the whole point.
The industry is converging on Kueue precisely because *GPU* capacity is scarce and expensive.
The same quota-and-priority machinery that keeps a claims batch fair is what stops a fleet of teams from fighting over GPUs.
A ResourceFlavor can describe GPU nodes; a ClusterQueue can hold `nvidia.com/gpu` quota; a high-priority evaluation job can preempt a low-priority training run.
Everything you learn here on tiny CPU Jobs is the same control plane that governs AI batch at scale — which is why distributed-training and model-evaluation stacks on OpenShift are built on Kueue.

In the lab you run a fraud-scoring inference Job against a *Models-as-a-Service (MaaS)* endpoint, through your LocalQueue, and watch it queue exactly like the statement run.
*AI batch is just batch.*

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Who owns the batch quota?"* is a real question with a real owner.
The ClusterQueue is the platform team's lever: it sets how much of the cluster the batch tier may use, and how teams share it.
Once that lever exists, "the month-end run took down the cluster" stops being a quarterly event.
* *Cron sprawl is technical debt.*
Teams pile up dozens of CronJobs nobody owns — half of them overlapping, some that should have become event-driven the day a queue appeared.
A CronJob you cannot name the owner of is a 2&nbsp;a.m. page waiting to happen.
* *AI workloads force the conversation whether you plan for it or not.*
The first time two teams want the same GPUs at once, someone invents an admission policy — usually a spreadsheet and a Slack argument.
Kueue is that policy written as code, and adopting it *before* the GPU crunch is far cheaper than during it.
