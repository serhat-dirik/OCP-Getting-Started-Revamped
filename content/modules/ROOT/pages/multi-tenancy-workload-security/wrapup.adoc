== What you built

You turned three empty projects into a governed *payments team* sandbox.
You met a workload that demanded root and watched *restricted-v2* reject it at admission — then fixed it in one line so it runs as a non-root UID from your namespace range, never root.
You gave `payments-ci` `edit` in dev and `view` in prod (one identity, two environments), then authored a custom `deployer` Role so `payments-ci`'s CI counterpart `payments-ops` can ship Deployments but is *blind to Secrets* — least privilege you wrote yourself.
You read the team's ResourceQuota, blew past it on purpose, read the refusal, and fixed it — and watched a LimitRange stamp defaults on a bare Pod.
Finally you turned the lens around and inspected how the platform provisioned *you*: a group binding for cluster-read, a scoped `admin` on your own projects, and walls you can't climb.

The one idea under all of it: *a safe sandbox is RBAC (who), quota (how much), and SCC (how) working together* — and every one of those guardrails belongs to a scope, so a tenant's mistake stays inside the tenant.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams get access today:

. *When a new engineer joins a team, how many places do you touch?*
You bound access to a *group*, so onboarding is one membership change.
In your org, is team access expressed as group bindings, or as a pile of per-person grants someone edits by hand?
If it's the latter, off-boarding is where it bites — the access nobody remembers to remove is the audit finding waiting to happen.
. *Who can read your production Secrets — and can you answer in one command?*
You ran `oc adm policy who-can get secrets` and got over a hundred subjects, then filtered to the ones that mattered.
For your most sensitive namespace, could you produce that filtered list on demand for an auditor?
If not, that's a `who-can` alias and a runbook entry with a real compliance value.
. *What happens when a legacy image that assumes root meets your cluster?*
You watched restricted-v2 refuse UID 0 and fixed the image instead of granting an exception.
When your teams migrate older workloads, is the default answer "fix the image," "grant `anyuid` and move on," or "nobody knows"?
The org that has a *policy* for that question — fix first, scope the exception to one ServiceAccount when you must — migrates securely; the one that doesn't accretes root grants it can never take back.

== When *not* to reach for this

Governance is a tool, and over-applying it is its own failure mode.
Be honest about the edges:

* *Don't write a custom Role when a built-in fits.*
`view`, `edit`, and `admin` cover the overwhelming majority of team access, and they're maintained for you as APIs evolve.
Write a custom Role only when you need a *specific* boundary the built-ins don't draw — like "deploy but never read Secrets."
A namespace full of bespoke Roles that merely re-approximate `edit` is maintenance debt, not security.
* *Don't reach for cluster-per-team when namespace-per-team is enough.*
Full cluster isolation multiplies cost and operational load by the number of tenants.
Most teams are perfectly isolated by RBAC + quota + NetworkPolicy in a shared cluster; step up to Hosted Control Planes or separate clusters only for a *real* boundary — hard regulatory separation, a blast radius you're contractually required to cap — not because it feels safer.
Density is a feature; pay for isolation only where you must.
* *Don't grant an SCC exception where fixing the image is cheaper.*
A scoped `nonroot-v2` grant to one ServiceAccount is legitimate for a workload that genuinely needs a fixed UID.
But most "needs root" is a Dockerfile that wrote to `/var` as UID 0 out of habit — fixable in minutes, and the fix is permanent.
Every SCC grant is a door you have to remember you opened; open as few as you can, and never `anyuid` when `nonroot-v2` would do.
* *Don't turn quota into a straitjacket.*
Quota exists to stop one team from starving the cluster, not to make engineers file tickets for every Pod.
Size it with real headroom and let LimitRange defaults do the paperwork; a quota so tight that teams spend their day fighting it is a platform team optimizing the wrong number.

== Go deeper

* *OpenShift Authentication & authorization* documentation — identity providers, the full RBAC reference, `oc adm policy` verbs, and the escalation-prevention rules you bumped into.
* *Managing Security Context Constraints* — the complete SCC field reference, the built-in SCCs (`restricted-v2`, `nonroot-v2`, `anyuid`…), and how Pod Security Admission and SCCs relate on OpenShift.
* *Networking for Dev & DevOps* (a separate module in this workshop) — the NetworkPolicy half of multi-tenancy: default-deny and precise allows, the network isolation that complements the RBAC and quota you built here.
* *Hosted Control Planes* — when namespace-per-team isn't enough and you want a per-tenant API server without a full cluster each; the stronger-isolation rung of the tenancy spectrum.
* *Securing Apps with Keycloak* (a separate module) — the application-authentication counterpart to this module's cluster-authentication: the same Red Hat build of Keycloak, serving app logins and, as you saw, cluster login too.

.Clean up
====
To return your world to ungoverned teammates and a not-yet-run workload — the module's entry state — run `ws prep {module-id} --yes`.
One caveat unique to this module: a plain reset purges workloads but *not* the RoleBindings and Roles you authored by hand (they harmlessly re-bind the same seeded ServiceAccounts).
`ws prep {module-id} --yes` re-materializes the entry state; to be fully pristine, delete the `edit`/`view`/`deployer` bindings you created (`oc get rolebinding -n {user}-dev`).
Leave everything running if you are continuing to another module — each module materializes its own world.
