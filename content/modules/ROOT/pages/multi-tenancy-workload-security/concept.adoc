[.lead]
Parasol is standing up a *payments team*: a few engineers, a CI robot, and three environments ‚Äî dev, stage, prod ‚Äî that must be theirs to move fast in, and nobody else's to break.
The platform team's job is to hand them exactly that: the right people with the right access, workloads that can't quietly run as root, and quota so one team's mistake can't starve the cluster.
This module is how OpenShift makes a *safe sandbox* ‚Äî RBAC for who-can-do-what, ResourceQuota and LimitRange for how-much, and the security defaults that keep a container honest.

== What you'll learn

* How *identity* becomes *authority* on OpenShift: the flow from identity provider ‚Üí user ‚Üí group ‚Üí RBAC, and why you bind to groups, not people.
* How to design *roles and bindings* for a team ‚Äî the built-in `view`/`edit`/`admin`, a *custom least-privilege Role*, and the escalation rule that stops a sandbox from escaping.
* How *ResourceQuota* and *LimitRange* shape what a namespace may consume ‚Äî and what happens the moment a workload asks for too much.
* Why your containers run *non-root with a random UID* by default ‚Äî Pod Security Admission and the *restricted-v2* Security Context Constraint ‚Äî and how a scoped exception is granted when a workload genuinely needs one.
* How *ServiceAccounts* and *bound tokens* give a workload a safe identity, and how the *tenancy spectrum* (namespace-per-team ‚Üí cluster-per-team) frames the bigger design choice.

== Identity, then authority

Every request to the cluster answers two questions in order.
First *authentication* ‚Äî who are you? ‚Äî settled by an *identity provider (IdP)* wired into OpenShift's OAuth server: an htpasswd file, an LDAP directory, or (as on this cluster) *Red Hat build of Keycloak*.
The IdP proves you are `alice`; it says nothing about what you may do.
Then *authorization* ‚Äî what may you do? ‚Äî settled by *RBAC*, which is the subject of most of this module.

The lever is *bind to the group, not the user*.
When a new engineer joins payments, you add them to the group and they inherit every permission the group already has ‚Äî no hunt through dozens of individual bindings.
This is not theory: *you* are governed exactly this way in this workshop.
Your login belongs to a `workshop-attendees` group; that group is bound to a read-only role for cluster-wide facts, and a separate per-user binding gives you `admin` on your own projects.
In the lab you will inspect those very bindings ‚Äî the platform already did to you what you are about to do to the payments team.

== The RBAC object model

RBAC has just four object types, and they compose in a way that is easy once you see the seam:

*Role* / *ClusterRole*::
A list of *permissions* ‚Äî "these verbs (`get`, `list`, `create`, `delete`‚Ä¶) on these resources (`pods`, `deployments`, `secrets`‚Ä¶)."
A `Role` is *namespaced*; a `ClusterRole` is *cluster-wide* and can be reused in any namespace.
A Role grants nothing until it is bound.
*RoleBinding* / *ClusterRoleBinding*::
Ties a Role or ClusterRole to *subjects* ‚Äî Users, Groups, or ServiceAccounts.
A `RoleBinding` grants the role's permissions *in one namespace*; a `ClusterRoleBinding` grants them *everywhere*.
The same ClusterRole (`view`) bound in two namespaces gives two independent, namespaced grants.

OpenShift ships three built-in ClusterRoles you will reach for constantly:

* `view` ‚Äî read most things in a namespace, *but not Secrets*.
* `edit` ‚Äî `view` plus create/change workloads, config, and routes ‚Äî *and it can read Secrets*.
* `admin` ‚Äî `edit` plus manage the namespace's own RBAC (Roles, RoleBindings) and ServiceAccounts.

`admin` sounds like "can do anything," and the honest surprise is that it *cannot*.
The built-in `admin` role deliberately *omits* applying a `ResourceQuota` or `LimitRange`, *omits* the cluster monitoring CRDs, and has *no rule for Security Context Constraints at all*.
That is not an oversight ‚Äî it is the *escalation-prevention* rule at work: RBAC forbids you from granting a permission you do not yourself hold.
A namespace admin who can't apply quota also can't lift their own quota; one who can't grant an SCC can't smuggle their workload to root.
The sandbox has walls precisely because "admin" is scoped, and you feel those walls in the lab.

== Quota and limits: the resource guardrails

RBAC governs *what actions* a subject may take; *ResourceQuota* and *LimitRange* govern *how much* a namespace may consume ‚Äî a different axis, owned by the platform team.

A *ResourceQuota* is a hard ceiling on a namespace: total `requests.cpu`, `requests.memory`, `limits.cpu`, `limits.memory`, and object counts (`pods`, `persistentvolumeclaims`).
The interplay that trips people up is *requests vs limits*: a *request* is what the scheduler reserves for a Pod (and what the quota's `requests.*` counts); a *limit* is the ceiling the kubelet enforces at runtime (counted by `limits.*`).
When a namespace has a quota on `requests.memory`, *every* Pod must declare a memory request, or it is refused.

That last rule would make the namespace tedious to use ‚Äî so a *LimitRange* fills the gap.
It supplies *default* requests and limits to any container that omits them, and can set floors and ceilings per container.
Quota sets the team's budget; LimitRange makes sure every Pod arrives with a price tag on it.
In the lab you read the quota you already live under, deliberately blow past it, read the refusal, and fix it ‚Äî then watch a LimitRange silently give a bare Pod its defaults.

== Workload security: non-root by default

Here is a fact that surprises everyone the first time: on OpenShift you *cannot* run a container as root just by asking.
The platform assigns each workload a *random, high-numbered, non-root UID* and rejects anything that demands otherwise ‚Äî unless a human has explicitly, narrowly, granted an exception.

Two mechanisms enforce this.
*Pod Security Admission (PSA)* is the upstream Kubernetes gate that labels namespaces `privileged`/`baseline`/`restricted`.
*Security Context Constraints (SCCs)* are the OpenShift control that actually decides what a Pod's `securityContext` may contain ‚Äî and on OpenShift, the SCC is the real enforcer.
The default SCC available to every workload is *restricted-v2*: it drops *all* Linux capabilities, forbids privilege escalation, requires a seccomp profile, and ‚Äî the beat of this module ‚Äî pins the UID to a range *assigned to the namespace*, which never includes 0.

That range is not abstract.
Each project carries an annotation, `openshift.io/sa.scc.uid-range`, and restricted-v2 draws the Pod's UID from it.
When a Deployment demands `runAsUser: 0`, admission compares 0 against that range and refuses the Pod ‚Äî the exact rejection you will read in the lab:

There are two honest resolutions, and the lab covers both.
The *right* fix almost always is *fix the image*: don't demand root ‚Äî most software doesn't need it, and a container built for OpenShift declares a numeric non-root user and group-writable paths (the discipline introduced when you built images earlier in the workshop).
When a workload *genuinely* needs a fixed or elevated UID ‚Äî a legacy database, a monitoring agent ‚Äî the platform grants a *scoped SCC exception*: a namespaced RoleBinding to a less-restrictive SCC (`nonroot-v2`, or `anyuid` only if root is truly required) targeting *one ServiceAccount*, never the whole namespace.
Narrow, justified, auditable ‚Äî the opposite of running everything as root.

== Workload identity: ServiceAccounts and bound tokens

People authenticate through an IdP; *workloads* authenticate as *ServiceAccounts*.
Every Pod runs as one (the namespace `default` SA if you don't choose), and RBAC governs a ServiceAccount exactly like a user ‚Äî which is why the payments team's CI robot is modelled as a ServiceAccount you bind a role to.

How a workload proves it *is* that ServiceAccount has changed for the better.
Older OpenShift auto-generated a long-lived token Secret for every SA ‚Äî a credential that never expired and sat in the namespace forever.
Current OpenShift turns that off: a fresh ServiceAccount has *no* token Secret.
Instead you mint a *bound token* on demand ‚Äî `oc create token <sa>` ‚Äî which is time-limited, audience-scoped, and (when mounted in a Pod) invalidated the moment the Pod is deleted.
In the lab you mint one and read its claims: a bounded lifetime and a subject that names exactly which ServiceAccount in which namespace.
Short-lived, scoped, revocable ‚Äî workload identity done right, and the same pattern an AI agent uses to reach the cluster with least privilege in a later module.

== Tenancy models: how far do you split?

"Multi-tenancy" is a spectrum of *blast-radius* choices, and OpenShift supports the whole range:

* *Namespace-per-team* ‚Äî many teams share one cluster, isolated by RBAC + quota + NetworkPolicy. Cheapest, densest, the default you are using; isolation is logical, so it depends on those guardrails being correct.
* *Virtual clusters / Hosted Control Planes* ‚Äî each tenant gets its own API server (its control plane running as Pods on a management cluster) while sharing worker capacity. Stronger isolation, a real option on OpenShift, without a full cluster per team.
* *Cluster-per-team* ‚Äî total isolation, total cost and operational multiplier. Reserved for hard regulatory or blast-radius boundaries.

Two cluster-wide levers decide how self-service the namespace-per-team model is, and both are the platform team's to set:
*self-provisioning* (by default, any authenticated user may create their own projects) and the *project request template* (what every new project is stamped with ‚Äî a default-deny NetworkPolicy, a starter quota).
Turning self-provisioning off and baking guardrails into a template is how a platform team moves from "anyone can make a wide-open namespace" to "every namespace is born governed."
Those levers are cluster singletons ‚Äî change one and it affects everyone ‚Äî so in the lab your instructor demonstrates them while you watch.

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Who can read the payment secrets?"* is a question a security auditor *will* ask, and `oc adm policy who-can get secrets` answers it ‚Äî except the raw answer on a real cluster lists *over a hundred* subjects (platform controllers, operators, every namespace's builders).
The skill is filtering to the subjects you care about; an audit you can't filter is an audit nobody runs.
* *"admin" is a scoped word, and teams learn it the hard way.*
The first time a team lead discovers their namespace `admin` can't raise their own quota, it feels like a bug; it is the platform protecting the shared cluster from every team's good intentions.
Naming that boundary up front ‚Äî quota is platform-owned, admin is namespace-scoped ‚Äî turns a support ticket into an architecture conversation.
* *The root-image fight is a migration tax nobody budgets for.*
Legacy images that assume UID 0 hit restricted-v2 on day one of a container migration.
The cheap path is fixing the image; the expensive path is a sprawl of `anyuid` grants that quietly re-open the door the platform closed.
Knowing the difference ‚Äî and that a *scoped* SCC to one ServiceAccount is the middle way ‚Äî is what keeps a migration secure instead of merely done.
