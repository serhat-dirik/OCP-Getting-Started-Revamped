== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams package and hand off software today:

. *When a colleague installs your service, what do you actually hand them?*
You handed over a versioned chart with an install, an upgrade, a test, and a rollback. If your teams hand over a folder of YAML each consumer edits by hand, every install drifts and no two are alike. Could a new team stand up your service with *one command* ŌĆö and could you tell them which *version* they're running?
. *When an upgrade goes wrong, can you get back?*
You broke a release on purpose and recovered with one `helm rollback`. If your deploys are `oc apply` over the top of the last one, there is no revision to return to ŌĆö the previous state is gone. Is "roll back the release" a command your teams can run, or a scramble to remember what the YAML used to say?
. *Do you know which version of a shared component is where?*
`helm history` and `helm list` make version and revision a fact. Many orgs cannot answer "which version of the shared auth chart is in team X's cluster" without archaeology. Is your answer a query, or a guess?
. *Have you written an operator you didn't need?*
You placed the notifications service on the spectrum and concluded *Helm*, not an operator. Teams reach for operators because they feel powerful, then maintain a reconcile loop a chart would have covered. Is there something in your estate that's an operator today but should have been a chart ŌĆö a controller with nothing to continuously reconcile?

== When *not* to reach for this

Packaging choices have failure modes of their own ŌĆö over-reaching is one:

* *Don't write an operator when Helm covers your day-2.*
The headline honesty rule. If your operational need is "occasionally upgrade with new values," a chart already does that. An operator adds a controller to build, certify, and keep alive for the life of the product ŌĆö pay that cost only when *continuous reconcile* (drift repair, stateful upgrade, failover, many-instances-from-one-spec) is the actual value.
* *Don't push charts to the app's image tag on the integrated registry.*
The internal registry maps OCI repos to `<namespace>/<name>`, so a chart pushed as `<namespace>/parasol-notifications` shares a repository with the app image. Keep them distinct by *version tag* (the chart's semver vs the image's tag), and on a general-purpose registry (Quay, Harbor) use a dedicated `charts/` prefix ŌĆö which the integrated registry does not accept.
* *Don't cram subcharts and CRDs into a chart to look thorough.*
A single-service chart with values, a Route, a test, and a hook is the shape most apps want. Subcharts (packaging *other* apps as dependencies) and shipping CRDs inside a chart are real features with real sharp edges ŌĆö reach for them when you genuinely package a *stack*, not to make one service look impressive.
* *Don't hand-roll an installer that reinvents Helm or OLM.*
A bash script that `oc apply`s in a loop with "if not exists" checks is an operator costume with none of the safety. If you need install/upgrade/rollback, that's Helm; if you need continuous reconcile, that's an operator. The imperative installer in between is the anti-pattern both were built to replace.
