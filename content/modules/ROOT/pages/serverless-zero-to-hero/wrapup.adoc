== What you built

You took Parasol's claims-processor from a *scaled-to-zero Knative Service* to a *tuned, split, and event-wired* one — without ever writing a Deployment, a Service, or a Route by hand.
You *observed* the `ksvc` Knative had already expanded into a Configuration, a Route, and a Revision, and read its *auto-created* HTTPS `status.url`.
You *woke it from zero* and *measured* the honest JVM cold start (~15 s) against a warm request (~0.1 s), and hit — then fixed — the cold-start-vs-client-timeout trap.
You *tuned* concurrency, minting a second revision, and watched the autoscaler climb *0 → 2 → 3* on concurrency and *melt back to zero*.
You *split traffic* 80/20 across two revisions by *tag*, addressed each revision through its own sub-route, and *rolled back* to 100% stable in one line.
And you wired a *taste of eventing* — a `PingSource` → in-memory `Broker` → `Trigger` — that woke the service *on an event*, meeting the honest `404` boundary where *processing* the event begins.

The one idea under all of it: *serverless turns "always-on capacity" into "pay for requests."* You describe *one* object — a Knative Service — and the platform owns the scaling decision all the way down to zero, mints an immutable revision on every change, auto-publishes the Route, and (with a trigger) wakes the service on an event. Rollout, rollback, and canary become *Route weights over revisions that already exist*.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your own services run today:

. *Which of your services sit idle a meaningful part of the day — and what do the always-on pods cost?*
You watched a service hold *zero* pods while quiet and wake on one request. For your internal tools, nightly feeds, or spiky endpoints, is the capacity reserved 24/7 for a load that only shows up part of the time — and would scale-to-zero give that reservation back?
. *When you say "cold start," is it a number or a fear?*
You *measured* it: ~15 s for this JVM service from zero, ~0.1 s warm. For your equivalent service, do you know the actual cold start — and for the paths that can't tolerate it, would a `min-scale` warm floor (or a natively-compiled build) be an acceptable trade?
. *Can you canary — and roll back — a new version without a redeploy?*
You shifted 80/20 and rolled back to 100% in *one line*, against a revision that already existed. Is that a one-command change in your environment, or a second-Deployment dance and a load-balancer ticket every time?
. *Do you have a place for event-driven work, or is everything a request?*
You wired source → broker → trigger and woke a service on an event. Where do your "when X happens, do Y" flows live today — cron jobs, polling loops, bespoke queues — and would a broker/trigger model be cleaner?
. *Would serverless actually fit — or are you reaching for it because it's new?*
The honest answer is a checklist (below). For each candidate service, is it bursty, idle sometimes, request- or event-driven, and cold-start-tolerant? If not all four, a plain Deployment may be the better tool.

== When *not* to reach for this

Serverless is powerful, and over-reaching with it is its own failure mode:

* *Don't put a steadily-busy service on scale-to-zero.*
If a service is always handling traffic, it never scales to zero anyway — so you get the cold-start risk and the extra autoscaler/ingress layer for *no* idle savings. A plain Deployment with an HPA is simpler and has no cold start. Serverless earns its keep on *bursty or idle* workloads.
* *Don't put a latency-critical path on a cold start you won't pay to avoid.*
A ~15 s first request is fine for a nightly feed and unacceptable for a checkout call. If the path is latency-critical, either keep a warm floor (`min-scale`, giving up scale-to-*zero*) or use a native build — and if neither is worth it, a normal Deployment is the honest choice.
* *Don't force a long-running or stateful workload into a request-driven model.*
Knative Serving is for *short, stateless* request handling. Long jobs, streaming, and stateful services fight the autoscaler and the request-timeout model — batch work is the Jobs/Kueue module, stateful work is the storage module. Use the right tool.
* *Don't mistake the eventing taste for eventing.*
The source → broker → trigger wiring you built *delivers and scales up*, but the service returned `404` because it doesn't *process* events. Shipping "we have eventing" on the strength of the wiring, without a real CloudEvents handler, delivery guarantees, filtering, and dead-letter handling, will disappoint the first team that needs them — that's the next module, on purpose.
* *Don't tune the shared autoscaler config for one service.*
The knobs you reach for are *per-`ksvc`* annotations (`target`, `min-scale`, `max-scale`) and `containerConcurrency`. The cluster-wide autoscaler ConfigMap affects *everyone* — leave it to the platform team and tune your own service.

.Serverless vs. Deployment — the honest decision matrix
[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Question | Lean serverless (`ksvc`) | Lean Deployment (+ HPA)

| Traffic shape | *Bursty / spiky / idle sometimes* | *Steady, always-busy*
| Latency budget | Tolerant of a cold start (or worth a warm floor) | Cold start unacceptable and no warm floor wanted
| Invocation | Request- or *event-driven*, short and stateless | Long-running, streaming, or stateful
| Rollout need | Canary/rollback as a *Route weight over revisions* | Standard rolling update (or Argo Rollouts for metric-gated)
| Cost model | *Pay for requests* — zero when idle | Pay for reserved capacity, busy or not
|===

== Go deeper

* *Knative Serving* — the `ksvc`, the Configuration/Route/Revision model, and the full autoscaler surface: soft `target` vs `containerConcurrency`, `min-scale`/`max-scale`, the stable window, and the scale-to-zero grace period.
* *Cold-start engineering* — warm floors (`min-scale`), natively-compiled runtimes (Quarkus native) for tens-of-milliseconds starts, and where the activator sits in the request path.
* *Traffic management on revisions* — tag-based blue/green and canary splits, per-tag sub-routes, and how this compares to Argo Rollouts (M10) and service-mesh routing (M18).
* *Knative Eventing* — sources, brokers, triggers, and the *CloudEvents* envelope: the model whose *processing* half is the *Eventing Deep-Dive & Serverless Workflows* module.
* *Knative Functions (`kn func`)* — language templates for HTTP and CloudEvents handlers, and the local-vs-on-cluster build paths (Podman + registry, or OpenShift Pipelines).
