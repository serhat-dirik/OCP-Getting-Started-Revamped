The one idea under all of it: *persistence is a property of the volume, not the app.*
The same database Pod lost or kept its data based only on what its `data` volume was — and a StatefulSet is the pattern for when each replica needs its *own* durable volume and a stable identity to go with it.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams run stateful workloads today:

. *Which of your "databases" are actually ephemeral?*
You just saw a Pod restart wipe a database whose storage was an emptyDir.
Somewhere in your estate is a dev or test datastore exactly like that — fine until the node it lands on drains.
For one real workload of yours: can you name its volume type without looking? If you can't, that is the thing to check on Monday.
. *Who owns backup and failover for your in-cluster data?*
A StatefulSet gives you identity and storage — not backups, not failover, not upgrades.
For each stateful workload you run in-cluster, who restores it when a volume is lost, and when did they last *test* that restore?
If the answer is "nobody has" then you are running a pet you cannot resurrect.
. *Is your StorageClass a clean contract?*
Your developers wrote a PVC and got 2Gi of storage without knowing or caring what array was behind it.
Do your app teams get that same self-service, or do they file a ticket and wait? The gap between those two is a platform-team backlog item with a real dollar value.

== When *not* to run it this way

In-cluster persistent storage and StatefulSets are powerful, and they are not always the right call.
Be honest about the edges:

* *A StatefulSet is not a managed database.*
For a production system of record, a managed data service (or a mature database Operator that owns failover, backup, point-in-time recovery, and minor-version upgrades) usually beats a hand-built StatefulSet.
Use the StatefulSet pattern when your team will genuinely own that operational burden, or when portability across clouds and staying inside one security perimeter outweigh the convenience of a managed service.
Running `replicas: 2` of PostgreSQL does *not* give you a replicated, highly-available database — those two Pods in the lab are independent instances; real replication is an Operator's job.
* *Reaching for ReadWriteMany is often a design smell.*
RWX is right for genuinely shared files — a media library, a shared content directory.
But teams frequently reach for it to work around a missing object store or a missing database, and shared-writer file volumes bring their own consistency and locking problems.
Before you require RWX, ask whether an S3-compatible bucket or a real datastore is the better shape — and remember RWX may not exist at all on some clusters.
* *Not every workload needs persistence.*
Caches, scratch space, and anything you can rebuild are *better* on emptyDir — persistent volumes cost money, count against quota, and are one more thing to back up.
The skill is knowing which data is precious and which is disposable, and only paying for the precious kind.

== Go deeper

* *Backup and restore (OpenShift API for Data Protection)* — persistence keeps data across Pod restarts, but not across a deleted PVC, a corrupted volume, or a bad migration. Backing up PVCs and namespaces is its own platform capability, covered in the data-protection module (a separate module in this workshop).
* *Jobs, Batch & Queued Workloads* — the batch module reuses a seeded PVC as shared input to parallel Jobs; it is a good next look at storage from the workload side (a separate module).
* Red Hat documentation: the OpenShift *Storage* guide (persistent storage, dynamic provisioning, expanding PVCs) and the *StatefulSet* / *Configuring persistent storage* sections; your storage backend's docs for the access modes it supports (this cluster runs OpenShift Data Foundation / Ceph).

.Clean up
====
To return your world to a fresh (ephemeral) claims app and remove everything you created — the claims-db PVC, the `pg-sts` StatefulSet and its per-Pod PVCs, and the init-container demo — run `ws reset {module-id}` in the Terminal.
Reset purges PersistentVolumeClaims explicitly, because StatefulSet volumes are *not* deleted when you delete the StatefulSet (you saw that in the challenge) and would otherwise leak.
Leave everything running if you are continuing to another module — each module materializes its own world.
