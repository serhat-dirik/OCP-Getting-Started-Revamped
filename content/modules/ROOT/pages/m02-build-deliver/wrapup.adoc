The idea under all of it: *the platform meets your app where it is.*
You never had to hand-write a Dockerfile for the common case — you handed OpenShift source and a builder image and it produced a running app; and when you needed control, the Docker strategy was right there.

== Map to your org

. *How many Dockerfiles do your teams maintain — and how many need to exist?*
You built a production Java service today with *zero* Dockerfiles, using S2I.
Pick a real service in your org: could it be built from source with a supported builder image, or does it genuinely need its own `Containerfile`?
What would it take to find out?
. *Where do your base images come from right now?*
You started from Red Hat UBI and a curated builder.
Trace one of your current production images back to its `FROM` line: who chose that base, when was it last patched, and could you prove where it came from if an auditor asked tomorrow?
. *Who curates what your developers can deploy?*
The catalog you used was curated — the Parasol PostgreSQL tile existed because someone replaced a bad default with a good one.
In your organization, is there a "paved road" of blessed builders, base images, and services — or does every team assemble their own, and rediscover the same mistakes?

== When *not* to do it this way

The build spectrum is about choosing the *right* on-ramp — which means being honest about when each is wrong:

* *Don't reach for a Dockerfile by reflex.*
If your app is a standard Java, Node.js, or Python service, a hand-written `Containerfile` is usually a liability you took on out of habit — a base image to patch and a build to maintain that S2I would have handled for free.
Use the Dockerfile strategy when you have a *reason*: a specific base image, a system package, a build step S2I cannot express.
* *Don't use S2I when you need to pin the exact image.*
S2I ties you to the builder image the platform provides.
When you must control the base image down to the layer — a hardened, minimal, or compliance-mandated base — the Dockerfile strategy is the honest choice, and owning that file is the price.
* *Don't run a single BuildConfig when the build is really a pipeline.*
The moment your build needs to test, scan, sign, and promote across environments, a lone BuildConfig is the wrong tool — that is what Pipelines are for (a later module).
This module is the single-build foundation, not the whole CI/CD story.
* *Don't treat this ephemeral database as real.*
The PostgreSQL you deployed loses its data when its Pod restarts.
That is fine for a dev sandbox and deliberately wrong for anything you care about — giving a database durable storage and identity is its own module (*Storage & Stateful Apps* — a separate module).
