== About this workshop

This is a hands-on OpenShift enablement workshop, built as a library of self-contained, story-driven modules.
It exists to take you from "I have credentials to a cluster" to "I can develop, deliver, and operate applications on OpenShift — and I understand *why* the platform works the way it does."
Every module is a lab you perform on a real cluster, not a slideshow: you deploy, break, fix, and observe things with your own hands.

By the end of the modules you work through, you will be able to deploy and scale an application, build and secure a delivery pipeline, adopt GitOps so that Git is the source of truth, promote a release safely across environments, protect an app with real identity, and troubleshoot it with metrics, logs, and traces.
The advanced modules go further — service mesh, serverless, eventing, resilience, application modernization, and running AI on the cluster — so you also leave knowing *when* to reach for each capability, and when not to.

== The Parasol story

Parasol Insurance has sold home and auto policies for decades, and its claims platform — the software adjusters use all day to open, assess, and settle a claim — *is* the business.
That platform grew up as a hand-tended application on servers that individual engineers named, patched, and nursed through the night.
It still runs, but every change is a gamble: deployments are manual and no two are quite alike, nobody can say exactly how the app is built, and the one team that guards the servers is a bottleneck for every other team.

The pain is equal parts technical and commercial.
There is no repeatable build and no shared record of how the service ships, so releases risk downtime and security gets bolted on late.
Meanwhile the business wants to move faster — new insurance products, an audit trail that satisfies regulators, and AI to help triage a growing claims backlog — and every one of those ambitions stalls against a delivery process nobody trusts.

You join Parasol's engineering organization to change that, and across the workshop you take the claims platform from its first container to a governed, observable, AI-assisted service on Red Hat OpenShift.
You begin with the *foundations*: deploy the claims app, scale and self-heal it, configure it across environments, and give its data a safe home.
You build the *delivery and trust* layer next — pipelines that ship the same way every time, a software supply chain you can prove, GitOps that makes Git the source of truth, and apps secured with real identity.
You take the *platform and tenancy* view after that, with many teams sharing one cluster under networking, scheduling, and image governance you design and defend.
Finally you reach for the *advanced* capabilities on Parasol's roadmap, from service mesh and serverless to modernization and the AI claims assistant that started the whole conversation.

Every module solves the next real problem Parasol hits, so each platform capability arrives as the answer to a question you already have — not a feature tour, but a story you build.

== How to use this workshop

This workshop is a *library of self-contained modules*, not a single track you must finish end to end.
Two things are true at the same time, and both matter.

*Any module can be your first.*
You never need to have completed an earlier module to start one.
When you begin a module, its world — namespaces, applications, Git repositories, pipelines, and access — is materialized for you on demand, so nothing depends on "please finish the previous module first."

*Following the numbers is still the recommended way through.*
The modules are numbered in teaching order: they build you up from foundations (deploy, scale, configure) toward more advanced topics (pipelines, platform tenancy, service mesh, AI), and each one assumes the *ideas* the earlier numbers taught — even though it never assumes their leftover cluster state.
If OpenShift is new to you, work through your session's modules in ascending order.
If you came for one specific topic, jump straight to it; it will stand on its own.

Your instructor selects the set of modules that fits your session — work through those, lowest number first.

=== Getting around the cockpit

Everything you need is on this screen: there is nothing to install and nothing to set up on your own machine.

Instructions:: The panel you are reading right now, on the left.
Terminal:: A live shell beside these instructions, already connected to the cluster as your user.
When a command shows a *run* control, it is click-to-run — click it and it executes in the terminal, so you never have to retype it (you can still type your own commands there any time).
Some modules give you *two* terminals so you can watch one thing while you do another: kill a pod in one and watch it heal in the other, or tail a pipeline's logs while it runs.
Web console & Gitea:: The OpenShift *web console* and *Gitea* — your Git server, holding your personal copies of the Parasol repositories — open as their own browser tabs, already signed in as you.
Links in the instructions deep-link you to the exact view you need.

You move among these constantly: read a step here, run it in the terminal or click through the console, then confirm the result.
A runnable command looks like the one below — click it, and the terminal prints your username:

[source,sh,role=execute]
----
oc whoami
----

That click-to-run loop is how you work through the entire workshop.

=== Prepare your environment

Each module's lab opens with a one-line command that prepares your environment for that module:

[source,sh,role=execute]
----
ws prep m01
----

`ws prep` checks your project, tells you if anything is left over from a module you ran earlier, and *asks before wiping* anything to set up the current module's world — so it is safe to run at any time.
Two companion commands help while you work: `ws verify m01` confirms your environment matches what the module expects, and `ws reset m01` returns it to a clean starting point.
