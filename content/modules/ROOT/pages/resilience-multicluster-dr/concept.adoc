[.lead]
Parasol's claims platform already shrugs off a crashing pod and a lost node — you built that reflex in Platform Orientation, Observability, and Deployment Targets & Scheduling.
But a whole *site* going dark — a data center, a region, or (in this lab) an entire namespace — is a different class of problem, and "we have three replicas" does nothing when the replicas are all in the site that just died.
This module is about the rungs *above* single-node resilience: designing an app to *fail over to another site automatically*, connecting services *across sites* without a VPN, and understanding the platform's *disaster-recovery (DR)* safety net underneath it all.

== What you'll learn

* The *resilience ladder* — pod → node → zone → *site* → region — and exactly which mechanism absorbs each rung, so you can say where "more replicas" stops helping and *cross-site failover* begins.
* How to make a service *survive in-site*: replicas, a *PodDisruptionBudget* (PDB), `topologySpreadConstraints`, and a *HorizontalPodAutoscaler* (HPA) — the primitives that turn a pod or node loss into a non-event.
* How *OpenShift Service Mesh* gives you *automatic cross-site failover* — a stable endpoint that routes to a primary site and, when that site fails, flips to a secondary *with no client change and no dropped requests*.
* How *Red Hat Service Interconnect* (RHSI) links *separate sites or data centers* into a single Virtual Application Network (VAN), so an app in one site consumes a service in another as if it were local — no VPN, no flat network `[ADD-ON]`.
* The *DR safety net* the platform provides — *OpenShift API for Data Protection* (OADP) backup/restore plus *GitOps re-materialization* (ties GitOps at Scale) — and the honest division of labor: the platform has your back for DR; *your* job as an app team is to design for resiliency and failover.

== The resilience ladder — where replicas stop and failover begins

Every resilience mechanism absorbs a *specific* failure. Stack them and you climb a ladder; the higher the rung, the bigger the blast radius and the more it costs to survive.

Read the ladder bottom-up.
The *green* rungs — a pod crash, a node drain, a zone loss — are *in-site resilience*, and you already own them: a Deployment's replicas and probes restart a dead pod (Platform Orientation); a PodDisruptionBudget, `topologySpreadConstraints`, anti-affinity, and an HPA keep a quorum alive through a node or zone loss (Observability, Deployment Targets & Scheduling).
The *amber* rung is this module's centerpiece: an *entire site* goes down — the data center loses power, the cluster fails, or (as we simulate here) the namespace is scaled to zero. No number of replicas *inside that site* helps, because the whole site is the thing that's gone. Surviving it means *failing over to another site* — and doing it *automatically*, so the client never notices.
The *blue* rung — a lost region — is where the platform's *DR* tooling (OADP + GitOps) comes in; we cover it as a concept, not a lab.

[NOTE]
.In-site resilience is a *prerequisite* for failover, not a substitute
====
Cross-site failover does not replace replicas, probes, PDBs, and spread — it sits *on top* of them.
A well-run service absorbs the common failures (rungs 1–3) automatically inside its own site, and only fails *over* to another site for the rare, whole-site catastrophe (rung 4).
If your app falls over on a single node drain, fix *that* first (Deployment Targets & Scheduling) — you are not ready to reason about cross-site failover while a node reboot takes you down.
====

== Designing a service to survive — in-site resiliency

Before a service can fail *over*, it has to be resilient *where it lives*. Parasol's claims service runs the Observability/Deployment Targets & Scheduling primitives, made concrete:

* *Multiple replicas* (three here) so losing one pod is a non-event — the Deployment replaces it in seconds.
* A *PodDisruptionBudget* that keeps a quorum (two of three) available during any *voluntary* disruption — a node drain evicts *at most one* replica at a time.
* `topologySpreadConstraints` + anti-affinity so the replicas land on *different nodes*, and one node loss can't take them all.
* A *HorizontalPodAutoscaler* that holds a replica floor and bursts under load.

That is the "design your app to survive" baseline. The lab starts by *proving* it — you kill a pod and watch the service never dip — and only then moves to the failure a resilient single site *cannot* survive: the loss of the whole site.

== Cross-site failover with Service Mesh — the centerpiece

Picture Parasol running its claims service in *two* sites (call them site A and site B). Site A is the primary; site B is a warm secondary. A client — a partner system, a mobile app, another service — calls the claims service through *one stable address* and must keep working *even if site A disappears*, without being reconfigured.

That is exactly what a service mesh gives you. OpenShift Service Mesh puts an *ingress gateway* in front of both sites as the *stable endpoint*, and programs three simple traffic rules that together produce *automatic failover*:

* A *ServiceEntry* declares one logical claims host whose endpoints are the two sites, each tagged with its *locality* (site A, site B).
* A *DestinationRule* turns on *locality-aware load balancing* (prefer the local/primary site) and *outlier detection* (eject a site whose requests start failing).
* A *VirtualService* on the gateway routes the stable endpoint to that host, with *retries* so a request that hits the just-failed site is transparently retried onto the healthy one.

The magic is that *nothing about the client changes*. It calls the same stable endpoint before, during, and after the failover. In the lab you watch a client hit that endpoint once a second and log which site served it: it reads `SITE=A` steadily, you *scale site A to zero* (the whole site "fails"), and within a few seconds the log flips to `SITE=B` — *every response still `200 OK`, not a single dropped request*. Restore site A and traffic returns to it. The mesh did the failover; you wrote three small resources and zero application code.

[NOTE]
.Why the client keeps getting `200`s during failover
====
Two mesh features cooperate. *Outlier detection* notices site A's requests failing and *ejects* it from the pool, so new requests go to site B. And *retries* catch the handful of in-flight requests that raced the ejection — a request that lands on the just-failed site is retried, lands on site B, and returns `200`. The client sees a clean flip from A to B, not an outage. That is the difference between "we have a second site" and "we fail over to it automatically."
====

== Cross-site connectivity with Red Hat Service Interconnect `[ADD-ON]`

Failover assumes the two sites can *reach* each other. When your sites are two namespaces on one cluster (as in this lab) that's free. When they are *two real data centers*, *two clusters*, or *a cluster and a partner's environment*, the old answer is a *VPN* or a *flattened network*: heavyweight, security-team-owned, and often impossible across organizational boundaries.

*Red Hat Service Interconnect* (RHSI), built on the open-source *Skupper* project, is the modern answer. It creates a *Virtual Application Network* (VAN): a dedicated, mutual-TLS (mTLS) Layer-7 overlay that links *specific services* across sites, over a *single outbound connection*, with no VPN and no shared subnet. Each participating site becomes a *Site*; you *expose* a service on one site with a *Connector*, *consume* it on another with a *Listener*, and *link* the sites by redeeming a short-lived token.

The app is *unchanged*: it connects to a *local* address (`claims-remote`) that only exists because the VAN provides it, and traffic is carried — encrypted — to the remote site's real service. In the lab you build the VAN between two "data centers" and watch a client in DC-A read DC-B's claims service (`SITE=B`) through that local name.

[IMPORTANT]
.Mesh vs. RHSI — two different jobs, on purpose
====
Keep these straight; they are *complementary*, not competing.
The *service mesh* governs *in-cluster* traffic — routing, mutual TLS, and the *primary/backup failover* between sites that share the mesh's ingress. It is your *traffic-policy and failover* layer.
*RHSI* is a *cross-site fabric* — it stitches *separate* clusters, data centers, or organizations into one VAN so services can find each other at all. It is your *connectivity* layer for spanning boundaries a single mesh doesn't cross.
A real multi-region Parasol would use *both*: RHSI to connect the regions, the mesh to fail over within the connected fabric.
====

== The platform's DR safety net — OADP + GitOps (concept)

Everything above is *your* job as an app team: design for resilience, wire up failover, connect your sites. Underneath it sits a *platform* safety net for the catastrophes even failover can't cover — a corrupted database, a fat-fingered delete, a whole cluster or region lost.

* *OpenShift API for Data Protection* (OADP), the supported packaging of the open-source Velero project, *backs up and restores* an application's Kubernetes objects *and* its persistent-volume data to an object store. When data is destroyed, OADP brings the *data* back — not just the empty objects. Backup/restore is a *cluster-admin / platform-team* operation; app teams *consume and verify* it.
* *GitOps re-materialization* (ties GitOps at Scale) is the quiet superpower. If your whole platform — namespaces, operators, apps, config — is declared in Git and reconciled by Argo CD, then "stand the environment back up on a new cluster" is not a heroic runbook; it is *point Argo CD at a fresh cluster and let it converge*. The *data* still needs OADP (or replication), but the *platform* rebuilds from Git in minutes.

[NOTE]
.The honest division of labor
====
The platform *has your back* for DR: OADP restores your data, GitOps rebuilds your platform, and *Advanced Cluster Management* (ACM) automates both across a fleet.
But that safety net only helps if *your app is designed to use it* — stateless where it can be, resilient in-site, and able to *fail over* to another site when one goes down.
This module's lab is the part that is *yours*: making the claims service survive a whole-site failure. The OADP/GitOps net is the concept underneath it — you'll see an instructor mention of a backup/restore, but the graded work is the failover you build.
====

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"We have a second data center."* — Do you *fail over to it automatically*, or is it a manual, 3-a.m. runbook? A warm site nobody has ever cut over to is a *hope*, not a capability. The lab's "scale the site to zero and watch the client keep working" drill is exactly the game-day every team should run.
* *"We have three replicas, so we're covered."* — Covered for a pod or node loss (rungs 1–3). *Not* covered for a whole-site outage. Name which rung each of your resilience measures actually absorbs, and the gap is almost always at the *site* level.
* *"Connecting the two sites means a VPN ticket and a three-week wait."* — Network-level coupling is where multi-site projects die. A service-level VAN (RHSI) links *just the services that need it*, over one outbound connection, crossing cluster and organizational boundaries a flat network never could.
* *"Our DR plan is a 40-page Word document."* — If recovery depends on humans executing a long runbook under pressure, your recovery time is whatever the most stressed engineer can manage. GitOps turns most of that document into "Argo CD converges the platform; OADP restores the data" — a plan you can *test* instead of *read*.
* *"Who owns resilience — the platform or the app team?"* — Both, at different layers. The platform owns the DR net (OADP, GitOps, ACM); the app team owns in-site resilience and failover design. Teams that blur the two either over-rely on a backup they never test, or build heroic app-level DR the platform already provides.
