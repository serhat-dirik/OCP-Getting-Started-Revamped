== What you built

You took Parasol's claims service from *resilient in one site* to *surviving the loss of a whole site* ‚Äî and proved it by scaling an entire site to zero and watching the client never notice.
You *inspected* the resilient primary ‚Äî three replicas, a `PodDisruptionBudget`, spread across nodes, a `HorizontalPodAutoscaler` ‚Äî and *killed a pod* to watch in-site resiliency absorb it.
You *wired cross-site failover* on OpenShift Service Mesh: a `ServiceEntry` declaring both sites with localities, a `DestinationRule` (locality-aware load balancing + outlier detection), and a `VirtualService` with retries on the ingress gateway ‚Äî then *failed the entire primary site* and watched the external client *flip from `SITE=A` to `SITE=B` with every request still `200`*, and *return* to A on recovery.
As a flagged `[ADD-ON]`, you linked two simulated *data centers* into a Skupper *Virtual Application Network* and read a remote site's service through a *local* address, with no VPN.
And you placed all of it on the *resilience ladder*: your job is in-site resilience and failover; the platform's job is the DR safety net (OADP + GitOps).

The one idea under all of it: *resilience keeps a site up; failover keeps the service up when a site goes down ‚Äî and they are different disciplines.* Replicas, probes, PDBs, and spread absorb the common failures *inside* a site automatically. For the loss of the *whole* site you need a *second site and automatic failover to it* ‚Äî which the mesh gives you for a few small resources and zero application code ‚Äî and, when the sites are real and distant, a *fabric to connect them* (RHSI). Under all of it sits the platform's DR net (OADP for data, GitOps for the platform) for the catastrophes even failover can't cover.

== When *not* to reach for this

Cross-site failover and cross-site connectivity are powerful, and mis-applying them is its own expensive failure mode:

* *Don't reach for failover when the failure is an in-site resilience gap.*
If your service falls over on a *single node drain*, the fix is a `PodDisruptionBudget` and spread (Deployment Targets & Scheduling), *not* a second site. Cross-site failover is for the loss of the *whole* site; using it to paper over rungs your replicas should absorb hides a bug you'll hit constantly.
* *Don't run a second site for a service that doesn't need one.*
A rebuildable cache or a stateless internal tool does not need a hot second site with automatic failover; a single resilient site (and the platform's backup net) is the right, cheap answer. Standing up cross-site failover for everything is a real and common over-spend ‚Äî reserve it for the services whose downtime genuinely costs.
* *Don't use RHSI where a plain Service or the mesh would do.*
A VAN is for linking services *across sites/clusters/organizations without a flat network*. For two services *in the same cluster*, a `Service` (and the mesh for policy) is simpler and faster ‚Äî reaching for an interconnect there adds moving parts for nothing.
* *Don't confuse "we have failover" with "we have DR".*
Failover keeps the service up when a site goes down; it does *not* bring back *corrupted or deleted data* ‚Äî for that you need OADP (or replication) and a *tested restore*. Failover + a DR net together are a strategy; failover alone leaves your data unprotected.
* *Don't let the failover primary flap.*
Tuning `outlierDetection` too aggressively (a tiny `baseEjectionTime`) makes the mesh re-probe a dead primary constantly, producing brief blips; too conservative and failback is slow. Size it to your outage profile, and *test it* ‚Äî a failover you've never triggered is untuned.

.Failover & connectivity decision guide ‚Äî what each tool is *for*
[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Need | Reach for | Not

| Survive a pod / node / zone loss | *Replicas + PDB + spread + HPA* (Observability/Deployment Targets & Scheduling) | A second site (wrong layer ‚Äî this is in-site resilience)
| Survive the loss of a whole site/cluster | *Mesh cross-site failover* (stable endpoint + locality + outlier + retries) | More replicas in the same site (they die with it)
| Connect real sites/DCs/clusters with no flat network | *RHSI / Service Interconnect* `[ADD-ON]` | A VPN or flattened network (heavier, cross-boundary-hostile)
| Recover destroyed or corrupted *data* | *OADP backup ‚Üí restore* (platform) | Failover (it moves traffic, not lost data)
| Stand a platform back up on a new cluster | *GitOps re-materialize* (Argo CD, GitOps at Scale) *+ OADP data* | A hand-built runbook
| Automate resilience + DR across a fleet | *Advanced Cluster Management* (ACM) | Per-cluster hand-rolling
|===

== Go deeper

* *Service Mesh failover* ‚Äî `localityLbSetting` `distribute`/`failover`/`failoverPriority` for explicit region‚Üíregion rules, active-active `distribute` weights, and the `outlierDetection` knobs (`splitExternalLocalOriginErrors`, ejection back-off) that shape how fast a site is ejected and re-admitted.
* *Red Hat Service Interconnect* `[ADD-ON]` ‚Äî Skupper `Site` high-availability (`ha: true`), multi-key `Listener`s, `Connector` load-balancing across a routing key, and *cross-cluster* links (the same CRs you used, pointed at a real second cluster instead of a second namespace).
* *OADP / Velero* (the DR net) ‚Äî `Backup`/`Restore` of an app's objects and persistent-volume data to an object store, `Schedule` for periodic backups, and the discipline of *drilling the restore* so a green backup is a tested capability, not a hope.
* *GitOps as DR* ‚Äî Argo CD app-of-apps re-materialization (GitOps at Scale): a Git-declared platform stands the DR site up as a convergence, not a runbook.
* *Advanced Cluster Management (ACM)* ‚Äî fleet-scale resilience + DR: cluster backup/restore built on OADP, cross-cluster networking (Submariner / RHSI), and GitOps-everywhere policy ‚Äî the platform-team's next step beyond this module.

[NOTE]
.The next real step ‚Äî make the failover a scheduled game-day
====
This module triggered *one* failover by hand. The natural next moves in a real Parasol build are to (1) *schedule a game-day* where you actually fail a site and confirm the cutover, (2) tune `outlierDetection` to your real outage profile so failback is neither flappy nor slow, and (3) pair the failover with the platform DR net ‚Äî OADP-restore drills and a GitOps-declared platform (GitOps at Scale) ‚Äî so you can recover not just from a site outage but from lost data and a lost cluster. A failover you've never triggered, like a backup you've never restored, is a hope, not a capability.
====
