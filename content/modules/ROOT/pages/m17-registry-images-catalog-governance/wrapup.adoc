== What you built

You took Parasol's claims image from *a tag in a registry* to *a governed part of a supply chain*.
You toured the internal registry — an in-cluster Service on `:5000`, backed by one volume, pruned nightly, with no external route — and read the seeded `parasol-claims` ImageStream resolving its `1.0` tag to an immutable `sha256` through a `referencePolicy: Local` pull-through.

You wired a *pull secret* to a workload two ways — linked to a ServiceAccount and named on a pod — the mechanics that authenticate to a private registry.

Finally you *read* the cluster-wide governance surface: where images may come from (`allowedRegistriesForImport`, IDMS/ITMS), what's retained (the nightly pruner), and what the catalog offers (the samples operator's 60 image streams and 51 templates, five catalog sources).

The one idea under all of it: *an image reference is a promise, and this whole layer is how you make it provable* — the promoted image is the one you tested (by digest), it can only come from a registry you allow, and the catalog offers exactly what the platform chose.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your teams' images and catalog are governed today:

. *When you "promote to prod," can you prove it's the image you tested?*
You promoted by digest and showed `prod` and `1.0` are the same `sha256`. If your promotion is a re-pushed tag, you're trusting nobody swapped it. Could you point at a production workload and prove — by digest — that it's the artifact that passed your tests, or only the tag that was pointed at it?
. *Where can images in your cluster come from — and who decided?*
`allowedRegistriesForImport` is an instant floor; `registrySources` and IDMS/ITMS are the heavier, disconnected-grade controls. Is there a written policy for which registries your clusters trust, or could a developer `import-image` from anywhere on the public internet today? For a regulated workload, "anywhere" is usually the wrong default nobody chose on purpose.
. *If your registry filled up overnight, would you know why — and would a build fail?*
The registry is one volume with a nightly pruner. On a long-running cluster, storage and retention are real operational concerns: a full registry blocks *every* push and build. Do you know your pruner's schedule and `keepTagRevisions`, or is retention something you'll discover during an incident?
. *Who curates your Developer Catalog — and is the "blessed starter" actually in it?*
You published a namespaced Template and read the cluster-wide samples operator. Many orgs leave the catalog at its defaults: full of samples they don't sanction, missing the one golden-path starter they do. Is your catalog a *decision* — skipped samples, a `ConsoleSample` or Template for the blessed path — or just whatever shipped?

== When *not* to reach for this

These controls are powerful, and over-reaching with them is its own failure mode:

* *Don't set `registrySources` or an IDMS/ITMS to solve an *import* problem.*
Those re-render every node and roll the pool — minutes of cluster-wide disruption. If you just want to stop *users* importing from an untrusted registry, `allowedRegistriesForImport` does it instantly with no reboot. Save `registrySources`/mirroring for pull-time enforcement and disconnected installs, where the node change is the point.
* *Don't disable samples with `managementState: Removed`.*
That deletes *all* 60 image streams and 51 templates at once — the catalog other modules depend on. To drop *one* sample, use the samples operator's `skippedImagestreams` / `skippedTemplates`. `Removed` is for a cluster that genuinely wants *no* Red Hat samples, not a scalpel.
* *Don't expose the internal registry with a route unless you need to.*
Turning on `defaultRoute` adds an internet-facing attack surface and TLS/pull-secret handling. If your workflow is in-cluster builds and pull-through (as this lab's was), the route-free posture is simpler *and* safer. Add the route only when an external client genuinely must push or pull directly — and then treat it like any other exposed endpoint.
* *Don't reach for a private-registry pull secret when pull-through would do.*
If the image lives in a registry the cluster already mirrors or caches (via `referencePolicy: Local` or an IDMS), workloads pull it locally with no per-workload credential. Reserve pull secrets for registries the cluster genuinely can't reach without authenticating — not as a default on every Deployment.

== Go deeper

* *Image configuration resources* — the `Image` config object: `allowedRegistriesForImport` (admission-level import control) and `registrySources` (node-enforced allow/block lists), including the field structure and blast radius you read.
* *Configuring image registry settings* and *Automatically pruning images* — the internal registry's storage, `defaultRoute`, and the `ImagePruner` (`schedule`, `keepTagRevisions`, `keepYoungerThan`) behind the nightly pruner.
* *Managing image streams* — tags, scheduled imports (`importPolicy.scheduled`), `referencePolicy: Local` pull-through, and image-change *triggers* that redeploy on a new digest.
* *Using image pull secrets* — `oc create secret docker-registry`, linking to a ServiceAccount `--for=pull`, and pod-level `imagePullSecrets` — for a real private registry or *Red Hat Quay*.
* *Mirroring images for a disconnected installation* — *ImageDigestMirrorSet* / *ImageTagMirrorSet*, the mirroring workflow, and `oc adm migrate icsp` (converting the deprecated predecessor) — the air-gapped supply chain.
* *Configuring the Cluster Samples Operator* and *Creating cluster samples (ConsoleSample)* — curating the catalog with `skippedImagestreams`/`skippedTemplates` and cluster samples; *Templates* for the namespaced catalog entry you published.
