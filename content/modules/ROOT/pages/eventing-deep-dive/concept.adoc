[.lead]
Parasol's claims-processor accepts a claim, and then *four* things must react — fraud-scoring, an audit record, a customer notification, an investigations referral — but if the app *calls* each of those services directly it is welded to all four: it waits on every one, a slow fraud service stalls claim submission, and a new listener means changing and redeploying the claims app.
*Eventing* breaks that weld: the app emits one *fact* — "a claim was submitted" — and moves on; the services that care *subscribe* to it, and the producer never learns who is listening.
This module builds that decoupled path for real on Red Hat OpenShift Serverless — a *broker*, attribute-filtered *triggers*, *retries and a dead-letter sink* for when a consumer fails, and the honest in-memory-versus-Kafka *broker-backing* decision — starting from exactly the eventing *taste* that M19 left you with.

== What you'll learn

* What *event-driven architecture* (EDA) is, the *source → broker → trigger → sink* model, and why emitting a *fact* decouples a producer from its consumers in a way a direct call never can.
* The *CloudEvents* envelope — the vendor-neutral shape (`type`, `source`, `id`, `specversion`, plus your own *extension attributes*) that makes an event routable and portable.
* How a *Trigger* routes by *attribute filter* — matching events are delivered, non-matching events are *dropped* — and how multiple triggers on one broker *fan out* independently.
* How Knative *survives a failing consumer*: delivery *retries* with backoff, and a *dead-letter sink* (DLQ) that catches the events that still fail — stamped with *why* they died.
* The *broker/trigger vs channel/subscription* decision, and the *in-memory vs Kafka* broker-backing decision — on *semantics* (durability, delivery, fan-out), not just definitions.
* Where *OpenShift Serverless Logic* (SonataFlow) fits for multi-step *workflows*, and the honest *choreography vs orchestration* choice — presented here as a concept and an `[ADD-ON]`.
* A runnable *EDA pattern catalog* — notify, event-carried state transfer, a glimpse of the saga — you can map onto your own systems.

== The problem eventing solves — decoupling

A direct call is a *command*: "claims-processor, call fraud-service and wait for a score." It is simple, and for a two-service, request-and-reply flow it is the right tool.
It becomes a liability the moment *more than one* thing cares about a claim, or when the caller shouldn't wait:

* *Coupling.* The claims app must know every downstream service — its address, its API, its health. Each new consumer is a code change to the *producer*.
* *Fragility.* If fraud-scoring is slow or down, claim submission is slow or down. The caller's fate is tied to the callee's.
* *Fan-out is manual.* Want audit *and* notification *and* investigations to react to a claim? The producer calls all three, in sequence, and owns the failure handling for each.

An *event* inverts this. The producer states a *fact* — "`com.parasol.claim.submitted`, here's the claim" — into a *broker*, and returns immediately. It does not know, and does not care, who consumes it.
Consumers *subscribe*: each declares, through a *trigger*, which events it wants. Fraud-scoring, audit, and notifications each get their own copy; a new analytics consumer is added by creating a *trigger*, with *no change to the claims app*.
That inversion — *the producer emits, the platform routes, consumers subscribe* — is the whole idea, and everything else in this module is a consequence of it.

[NOTE]
.The honest source — and why it doesn't weaken the lesson
====
There is one honest boundary, and it is the same shape as M19's `404`. In this module the *source* of events is a *seeded ticker* — a Knative *PingSource* that emits a claim-shaped event on a schedule — *not* a real `POST /api/claims` on the app. That is because `parasol-claims` does not yet *emit* CloudEvents; giving it an emit handler is net-new application work, out of scope here.
What is *real* is everything downstream of the source: the broker, the triggers, the attribute filtering, the consumer that genuinely *receives, validates, and displays* each CloudEvent, the retries, and the dead-letter sink. You will also send your *own* CloudEvents by hand — exactly as a real producer would — and watch them route, filter, wake a consumer from zero, and dead-letter on failure. Repointing the triggers from the seeded `dev.knative.sources.ping` events to real `com.parasol.claim.submitted` events is *one app change away*, and the wrap-up says exactly where it goes. Read the seeded source as "the plumbing and processing are real; the app-side emit is the next step," not as a shortcut.
====

== The four primitives — source, broker, trigger, sink

Knative Eventing is four moving parts, each a Kubernetes *custom resource* (CR):

* A *Source* emits events. You have a *PingSource* (`sources.knative.dev/v1`) that emits a claim-shaped event on a schedule — the seeded producer.
* A *Broker* (`eventing.knative.dev/v1`) is the *hub*: it receives events and holds them for routing. It has an address; producers POST CloudEvents to it. The default is *in-memory* — no external messaging system required.
* A *Trigger* (`eventing.knative.dev/v1`) is a *subscription with a filter*: "deliver broker events matching *these attributes* to *this sink*." Triggers are the routing layer.
* A *Sink* is anything *addressable* that receives events — here, a *Knative Service* (`ksvc`) consumer that scales from zero *on an event*.

The *amber* source feeds the *blue* broker; the *green* triggers each decide, by *attribute filter*, which events they want; the *light-blue* sinks receive and process. The producer (you, or the seeded ticker) POSTs to the broker and gets an `HTTP 202` — "accepted" — and never touches a consumer directly. That indirection is the decoupling made concrete.

== The CloudEvents envelope — why events are routable

An event isn't just a JSON blob. It is a *CloudEvent*: a small, vendor-neutral envelope (a Cloud Native Computing Foundation specification) that wraps your data with *attributes* every eventing system understands. When the consumer receives one, it displays exactly this:

[source,texinfo]
----
☁️  cloudevents.Event
Validation: valid
Context Attributes,
  specversion: 1.0
  type: com.parasol.claim.submitted
  source: /demo/claims
  id: claim-9001
  datacontenttype: application/json
Data,
  { "claimId": "CLM-9001", "amount": 7300, "fraudScore": 0.91 }
----

The *context attributes* are what make routing possible:

* `type` — *what happened* (`com.parasol.claim.submitted`). This is the primary thing triggers filter on.
* `source` — *who emitted it* (a URI-ish identifier of the producer).
* `id` — unique per event, for de-duplication and tracing.
* `specversion` — the CloudEvents version (`1.0`).
* *Extension attributes* — your *own* fields promoted into the envelope. The seeded source stamps `claimpriority: high`, so a trigger can route on *business* meaning, not just the technical `type`.

Because the routable facts live in the *envelope*, a trigger never has to parse your `Data` to decide where an event goes. That is why attribute filtering is cheap and why CloudEvents is portable across brokers, languages, and clouds.

== Broker/Trigger vs Channel/Subscription — a real decision

Knative gives you *two* ways to move events, and choosing between them is a semantics decision, not a style one.

[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| | *Broker + Trigger* | *Channel + Subscription*

| Shape | A *hub* many triggers attach to | A *pipe* with ordered subscribers
| Routing | *Attribute filtering* per trigger (`type`, extensions) | *No filtering* — every subscriber gets every event
| Fan-out | Natural: N triggers, each independent | Fan-out by adding subscriptions; no per-subscriber filter
| Best for | *Event distribution* — "route claims by type/priority to the right consumers" | *Linear pipelines* — "transform, then forward, then forward"
| Replies | A trigger's sink can *reply* an event back to the broker | A subscription has an explicit `reply` channel
|===

The rule of thumb: reach for *Broker + Trigger* when you are *distributing events to many interested consumers with filtering* — which is almost always the app-integration case, and it is what this module builds. Reach for *Channel + Subscription* when you have an *ordered, linear processing pipeline* and want explicit control of each hop. A broker is, in fact, *implemented* on a channel — so this is a question of the *abstraction you program against*, not two unrelated systems.

== Filtering, delivery, and the dead-letter sink

Two trigger capabilities turn "events flow" into "events flow *reliably and selectively*."

*Attribute filtering* is an *include rule*. A trigger with `filter.attributes.type: com.parasol.claim.submitted` delivers *only* matching events; everything else it *drops*. Multiple triggers filter *independently* on the same broker, so one event can match several triggers (fan-out) or none (dropped). A non-matching event is not an error — it simply isn't this trigger's concern, and it doesn't even wake this trigger's consumer.

*Delivery guarantees* answer the harder question: what happens when a consumer *fails*? A trigger's `delivery` spec adds two things:

* *Retries* — on a failed delivery, Knative retries `retry: N` times with a *backoff* (exponential is the sane default), giving a briefly-unhealthy consumer time to recover.
* A *dead-letter sink* (DLQ) — when the retries are exhausted, the event is *not dropped*; it is delivered to a separate sink you nominate, stamped with *why* it died. On this cluster, a dead-lettered event arrives with extension attributes like `knativeerrorcode: 404` and `knativeerrordest` (the address that kept failing) — so the dead-letter sink is a *diagnosable record*, not a black hole.

[NOTE]
.The in-memory broker is *synchronous* — a property, not a bug
====
The default broker (`MTChannelBasedBroker`) is backed by an *in-memory channel*. Two consequences you will *see* and should understand:

* *Delivery is synchronous end-to-end.* When you POST a claim to the broker, the `202` comes back only after delivery (and any retries) *complete*. A consumer that is scaled to *zero* must *cold-start* first, so the very first POST can take ten-plus seconds to return `202` — the consumer is waking, exactly the M19 cold start, now on the delivery path. Warm, it returns in a fraction of a second.
* *It is not durable.* In-memory means "gone if the broker pod restarts." That is fine for dev, demos, and best-effort internal events — and it is *why* the durable, buffered, replayable *Kafka* broker exists for events you cannot afford to lose.

Both points are the setup for the broker-backing decision below.
====

== Choosing the broker backing — in-memory vs Kafka `[ADD-ON]`

The core of this module runs entirely on the *in-memory* broker that Knative Eventing ships — no extra subscription, no middleware. That is the right default for most app-integration eventing.

When you outgrow it, the answer is a *Kafka-backed broker*, provided by *Streams for Apache Kafka* {streams_kafka_version} — a *separate subscription* (`[ADD-ON]`), so the module never requires it.

[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| | *In-memory broker* (default) | *Kafka broker* `[ADD-ON]`

| Durability | *None* — lost on restart | *Persistent* — events survive restarts
| Replay | No | Yes — consumers can re-read
| Ordering | Best-effort | Per-partition ordering
| Cost | Free with Serverless | A Streams subscription + operational weight
| Reach for it when | Dev, demos, best-effort internal events | Events you *cannot lose*, high throughput, replay/audit
|===

The honest guidance: *default to in-memory; move to Kafka when the events are financially or legally consequential and losing one is unacceptable.* "We have eventing" on an in-memory broker is fine until the first dropped payment event — and then it is a separate subscription and a real migration, not a config flag. The `[ADD-ON]` section of the lab shows the swap and the durability contrast for audiences who own Streams.

== Workflows — SonataFlow, and choreography vs orchestration `[ADD-ON]`

Triggers give you *choreography*: each consumer reacts to events on its own, with no central conductor. It is beautifully decoupled, and it is *hard to reason about* once a business process has branches, retries across steps, and timeouts — "did the claim get approved, or is it stuck waiting on a step three services away?"

*Orchestration* answers that with a *conductor*: one component owns the process, calls services in order, branches on data, and handles timeouts. On OpenShift that conductor is *OpenShift Serverless Logic* {serverless_logic_version} — a *SonataFlow* runtime implementing the Cloud Native Computing Foundation *Serverless Workflow* specification, running *on* Knative. A Parasol claims-approval workflow would auto-approve claims below a threshold, route the rest to a review service, and take a *timeout* branch if review doesn't answer in time — as one declarative workflow with a queryable instance per claim.

[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| | *Choreography* (triggers) | *Orchestration* (SonataFlow)

| Control | *Distributed* — each service reacts | *Centralized* — one workflow conducts
| Visibility | "What reacted?" is emergent | One place shows each instance's state
| Best for | Loose fan-out, notifications, decoupling | Multi-step processes with branches + timeouts
| Cost | Just triggers | A workflow runtime + its own state store
|===

[NOTE]
.Why SonataFlow is a concept + `[ADD-ON]` here, not a graded exercise
====
OpenShift Serverless Logic is *generally available* (since OpenShift Serverless 1.33; operator {serverless_logic_version}) with a console, a `kn-workflow` CLI, and a VS Code extension — it is real and production-ready. It is presented here as a *concept and an `[ADD-ON]`* for one honest reason: a per-user SonataFlow platform (the workflow runtime plus its own state database) is *significant footprint* to stand up for every attendee, and the graded core of this module is the *eventing* substrate. The wrap-up points you at the workflow story and the decision — choreography for decoupled fan-out, orchestration when a *process* needs a conductor — without asking every seat to run the platform.
====

== EDA patterns you'll recognize

Three patterns cover most real event-driven systems, and you build the first two here:

* *Notify* — "something happened; whoever cares, react." A `claim.submitted` fact fanning out to fraud, audit, and notifications is pure notify. Cheapest, most decoupled, no reply expected.
* *Event-carried state transfer* — the event carries *enough data* (the claim itself) that a consumer needn't call back to the producer for details. Your CloudEvents carry the claim in `Data`, so the consumer is self-sufficient — that is this pattern.
* *Saga (a glimpse)* — a multi-step transaction across services, coordinated by events, where each step has a *compensating* action if a later step fails. This is where *orchestration* (SonataFlow) earns its keep; the module points at it rather than building a full saga.

== How this shows up in real orgs

* *"Every time we add a consumer, we change the producer."* That is the coupling tax, and eventing is the fix: emit a fact, let consumers subscribe with triggers. A new analytics or compliance listener is a *trigger*, not a producer redeploy.
* *"A slow downstream service stalls our main flow."* Direct calls tie the caller's latency to the callee's. Emitting an event returns immediately; the consumer processes on its own clock, and if it is briefly down, retries and the dead-letter sink hold the line instead of failing the user.
* *"We route events with `if` statements in application code."* Attribute filtering moves routing *out* of your app and into declarative triggers — route by `type` or a business `claimpriority`, change the routing by editing a trigger, no code deploy.
* *"How do we know we're not silently losing events?"* That is the dead-letter sink's whole job: exhausted events land in a diagnosable place stamped with *why*, so "we lost some events and didn't notice" becomes "here are the dead events and their error codes."
* *"Do we need Kafka?"* Sometimes — and the honest answer is a *decision*, not a default. In-memory eventing is free with Serverless and fine for best-effort; Kafka is a separate subscription for events you cannot lose. Know which kind you have *before* you pick.
