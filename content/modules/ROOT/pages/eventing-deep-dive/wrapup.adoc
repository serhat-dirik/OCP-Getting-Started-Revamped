== What you built

You took Parasol's claims flow from a *taste* of eventing to a *real, decoupled, failure-surviving* event pipeline ‚Äî without the producing app ever knowing who consumes its events.
You *inspected* the four primitives ‚Äî a seeded `PingSource`, an in-memory `Broker`, a baseline `Trigger`, and a scale-to-zero consumer ‚Äî and confirmed the `source ‚Üí broker ‚Üí trigger ‚Üí sink` loop runs unattended.
You met a *real CloudEvents consumer* that returns `HTTP 200`/`204` and *displays the envelope* ‚Äî the honest difference from M19's `404`.
You POSTed CloudEvents to the *Broker* and watched a trigger route them, *waking the consumer from zero on an event*.
You *routed by attribute filter* ‚Äî proving, by match count, that matching events are delivered and non-matching are *dropped*, independently, per trigger.
You made delivery *survive failure* ‚Äî retries with backoff and a *dead-letter sink* ‚Äî then *broke* the consumer and read `knativeerrorcode: 404` off the dead event Knative refused to lose.
And you met the decisions that travel with you: *broker vs channel*, *in-memory vs Kafka*, and *choreography vs orchestration*.

The one idea under all of it: *emitting a fact decouples a producer from its consumers.* You describe *one* event ‚Äî a CloudEvent with a routable envelope ‚Äî and the platform owns delivery: triggers filter it to exactly the consumers that care, retries and a dead-letter sink keep it from being silently lost, and a new consumer is a *trigger*, never a producer change. Routing, fan-out, and failure handling become *declarative infrastructure*, not application `if` statements.

== Map to your org

Take these back to how your own services integrate today:

. *Where does adding a consumer force a change to the producer?*
You added fraud-review and audit routing without touching the event source. In your systems, when a new team needs to react to "an order shipped" or "a claim submitted," do they get a *trigger* ‚Äî or a change request against the producing service and a redeploy?
. *Which of your synchronous calls shouldn't be synchronous?*
You saw a producer emit a fact and return immediately, with the consumer processing on its own clock. Which of your call chains fail the user because a *downstream* service is slow or down ‚Äî and would emitting an event (with retries and a dead-letter sink behind it) break that coupling?
. *Where is your routing logic ‚Äî in triggers, or in `if` statements?*
You routed by `type` and by a business `claimpriority` attribute, changing routing by editing a trigger. In your apps, how much routing lives in application code that has to be redeployed to change ‚Äî and could it move into declarative filters?
. *How would you know you were silently losing events?*
You read a dead event's `knativeerrorcode`. Do your event flows have a dead-letter sink and an alert on it ‚Äî or would a failing consumer drop events with no one noticing until a customer calls?
. *Which of your events actually need Kafka ‚Äî and do you know it's a separate subscription?*
You ran everything on the free in-memory broker and saw why it's not durable. For each of your event types, is it best-effort (in-memory is fine) or unloseable (Kafka, a Streams subscription)? Deciding *before* the first dropped payment event is the point.

== When *not* to reach for this

Eventing is powerful, and over-reaching with it is its own failure mode:

* *Don't event-ify a simple request/reply.*
If a caller needs an *answer now* ‚Äî "score this claim and give me the number" ‚Äî a direct, synchronous call is simpler and clearer than emitting an event and correlating a reply. Eventing shines for *notify-and-fan-out* and *decoupling*, not for RPC with a return value. Using a broker where a function call would do adds latency and moving parts for nothing.
* *Don't ship "we have eventing" on an in-memory broker for events you can't lose.*
The in-memory broker is *not durable* ‚Äî a broker restart drops in-flight events. That's fine for best-effort internal signals and a disaster for a payment or a legal record. If losing an event is unacceptable, that's a Kafka broker (a separate subscription), not a config tweak ‚Äî decide it up front.
* *Don't skip the dead-letter sink and call it done.*
A trigger without a `deadLetterSink` *drops* events once retries exhaust ‚Äî silently. If the events matter enough to route, they matter enough to dead-letter and alert on. "It worked in the demo" hides the failure path; wire the DLQ before you rely on the flow.
* *Don't reach for orchestration when choreography is enough ‚Äî or the reverse.*
A simple "when X happens, notify Y and Z" is *choreography* (triggers) and needs no workflow engine. A multi-step business process with branches, cross-step retries, and timeouts is *orchestration* (SonataFlow) and will be a tangle of triggers if you force it into choreography. Match the tool to the shape of the process.
* *Don't confuse a broker with a channel.*
A *Broker + Triggers* is for filtered fan-out; a *Channel + Subscriptions* is for a linear pipeline. Using a channel where you need per-consumer filtering means routing logic leaks back into your consumers.

.Eventing decision guide ‚Äî what each tool is *for*
[cols="1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Need | Reach for | Not

| React to a fact, fan out to many, filter per consumer | *Broker + Triggers* (this module) | A channel (no filtering) or direct calls (coupling)
| An ordered, linear processing pipeline | *Channel + Subscriptions* | A broker (fan-out you don't need)
| Events you cannot afford to lose | *Kafka broker* `[ADD-ON]` (Streams subscription) | In-memory (not durable)
| A multi-step process with branches + timeouts | *Orchestration* ‚Äî SonataFlow `[ADD-ON]` | A web of triggers (choreography that's hard to trace)
| A step-by-step CI/CD build-and-deploy | *Pipelines* (M07) | Eventing (wrong tool)
| Bulk/parallel work with admission control | *Jobs + Kueue* (M06) | Eventing (wrong tool)
| An answer *now* from one service | A *direct synchronous call* | Eventing (latency + reply correlation for nothing)
|===

== Go deeper

* *Knative Eventing* ‚Äî the full `source ‚Üí broker ‚Üí trigger ‚Üí sink` model, the `Sources` catalog (`ApiServerSource`, `SinkBinding`, `KafkaSource`), and `Trigger` filters beyond exact-match (the CloudEvents SQL/`cesql` expression filter).
* *CloudEvents* ‚Äî the envelope spec, binary vs structured content modes, and extension attributes as first-class routing keys.
* *Delivery guarantees* ‚Äî `retry`, `backoffPolicy`/`backoffDelay`, `deadLetterSink`, and *draining* a dead-letter sink (re-processing dead events once a consumer is healthy).
* *Kafka-backed eventing* `[ADD-ON]` ‚Äî the `KnativeKafka` custom resource, the Kafka broker class, `Topic` CRs, Quarkus producer/consumer, and KEDA lag-based scaling ‚Äî on *Streams for Apache Kafka* {streams_kafka_version} (a separate subscription).
* *OpenShift Serverless Logic (SonataFlow)* {serverless_logic_version} ‚Äî the CNCF Serverless Workflow spec on Knative: branching on event data, calling services, timeout branches, the workflow console, and the `kn-workflow` CLI.

[NOTE]
.The next real step ‚Äî make the *app* emit events
====
This module's *source* was a seeded `PingSource` ticker, on purpose: `parasol-claims` doesn't yet *emit* CloudEvents. The natural next move in a real Parasol build is to give the app an *emit handler* ‚Äî on `POST /api/claims`, emit a `com.parasol.claim.submitted` CloudEvent to the broker (a Quarkus reactive-messaging or CloudEvents-SDK change) ‚Äî and then *repoint your triggers* from the seeded `dev.knative.sources.ping` events to the real `com.parasol.claim.submitted` events. Nothing you built changes: the broker, the filters, the retries, the dead-letter sink, the consumers all keep working ‚Äî only the *source* becomes real. That's the honest boundary of this module, and it's one app change from closed.
